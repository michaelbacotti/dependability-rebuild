SPX vs QQQ Iron Condors: Instrument Comparison, Sizing, and Margin

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The S&P 500 Index (SPX) and the Invesco QQQ Trust (QQQ) are the two most popular underlyings for iron condors. Both are highly liquid, both have tight bid-ask spreads, and both offer a range of expirations suitable for premium collection. But they differ meaningfully in contract size, margin treatment, tax classification, and how the underlying behaves during market events. The choice between SPX and QQQ condors is not about which is "better"; it is about which fits the account type, the capital base, and the trader's view on diversification.

Contract Mechanics

SPX is a cash-settled index option. Each contract has a $100 multiplier. A 15-point wide SPX condor with a $1.80 credit has the following per-contract values:

  • Max profit: $1.80 × $100 = $180
  • Max loss: $13.20 × $100 = $1,320
  • BPR (Reg-T): ~$1,500 - $3,000 depending on broker

QQQ is an ETF option that settles into shares of QQQ. Each contract has a 100-share multiplier. A $1.00-wide QQQ condor with a $0.40 credit on a $450 underlying has the following per-contract values:

  • Max profit: $0.40 × 100 = $40
  • Max loss: $0.60 × 100 = $60
  • BPR (Reg-T): ~$60 - $100

The dollar per-contract risk on QQQ is much smaller than on SPX. A 1-contract QQQ condor carries $60 of max risk; a 1-contract SPX condor carries $1,320 of max risk. For position-sizing purposes, QQQ condors are sized in larger contract counts (10-50 contracts) and SPX condors in smaller counts (1-5 contracts).

Margin Requirements

Account Type SPX BPR per Contract QQQ BPR per Contract
Cash account Full cash settlement Full cash equivalent (strike × 100)
Reg-T margin $1,500 - $3,000 $60 - $200 (cash-secured)
Portfolio margin ~$500 - $1,500 ~$30 - $100
IRA / retirement Not allowed (cash-settled) Allowed (assigned to shares)

The IRA restriction on SPX is significant. Cash-settled index options cannot be held in retirement accounts, which means IRA traders who want to run condors must use QQQ (or another ETF option like IWM or SPY). The choice of underlying is therefore partly determined by account type.

Liquidity and Spreads

Both SPX and QQQ have excellent liquidity, but SPX is structurally deeper. SPX has multiple expiration cycles (daily, weekly, monthly, end-of-month, end-of-quarter) and tighter bid-ask spreads on the most active strikes. QQQ has weekly and monthly expirations but the spread on less-liquid strikes can widen.

Metric SPX (15-pt wide, 45 DTE) QQQ ($1 wide, 45 DTE)
Bid-ask spread on short strike $0.05 - $0.10 $0.02 - $0.05
Open interest at popular strikes 50,000+ 20,000+
Volume on expiration Friday 100,000+ 50,000+
Average daily move (30-day) 0.8% - 1.2% 1.0% - 1.5%

For most retail traders, both SPX and QQQ offer sufficient liquidity. The bid-ask spread differences are usually small relative to the credit collected, and the volume is high enough to enter and exit positions without slippage.

Underlying Behavior

SPX and QQQ are correlated (the correlation is typically 0.85-0.95 over rolling 30-day windows) but they have different volatility profiles. QQQ has higher realized volatility than SPX because it is concentrated in technology stocks, and the volatility is more sensitive to single-stock news (e.g., a major earnings miss by an Apple, Microsoft, or Nvidia).

Metric SPX QQQ
30-day realized volatility (typical) 12% - 18% 15% - 22%
Average daily move 0.5% - 1.0% 0.7% - 1.3%
Skew (OTM put IV vs OTM call IV) Modestly positive More positive (tech-heavy)
Tail risk (2008, 2020 magnitude moves) 5-7% in a day 7-10% in a day

The higher volatility of QQQ means that the wing width needs to be larger in percentage terms to give the position the same range tolerance. A 15-point SPX wing at 5,800 is 0.26% from the current price; a $1.50 QQQ wing at $450 is 0.33% from the current price. The condor trader needs to size the QQQ wing to give the position a comparable range.

Strike Selection

For SPX, the standard 15-20 point wing is calibrated to a 0.25-0.35% range. For QQQ, the equivalent wing is $2.00 - $3.00 per side (0.45-0.65% range) because the underlying's daily moves are larger.

The QQQ condor can use smaller percentage wings if the position is being run on a stock that has a tighter realized volatility than the broader QQQ basket. For example, a condor on a single technology stock (AAPL, MSFT) might use a 5% wing because the stock's volatility is well-defined.

Tax Treatment

This is one of the largest practical differences between SPX and QQQ condors.

SPX is a Section 1256 contract. Section 1256 contracts receive special tax treatment:

  • 60% of gains are taxed at the long-term capital gains rate
  • 40% of gains are taxed at the short-term capital gains rate
  • This applies regardless of the holding period
  • Mark-to-market accounting applies at year-end

QQQ is a Section 1256 contract as well (since 2022 changes), but for traders using older tax frameworks, QQQ options have historically been treated as Section 1256 contracts under most conditions. The exact classification depends on the holding period and the contract type.

For most retail traders running premium-collection strategies, both SPX and QQQ receive similar tax treatment under Section 1256. The advantage of SPX is the cash settlement — there is no risk of being assigned on a short option and having to take delivery of shares, which can complicate tax reporting for QQQ in some scenarios.

Settlement and Assignment Risk

SPX is cash-settled. At expiration, in-the-money options are automatically settled for cash. There is no risk of being assigned shares of an ETF, no early exercise complications, and no need to manage the underlying.

QQQ is an ETF option that can be assigned. If a short option is in the money at expiration (or in some cases, early exercise scenarios), the trader may be assigned 100 shares of QQQ per contract. For a cash account, this means the trader must have $45,000 in cash available per assigned contract. For a margin account, the shares are simply delivered to the account.

The assignment risk is not large in practice — most condors are closed before expiration, and the broker typically auto-cashes short options that are $0.01 or more in the money — but it is a logistical complication that does not exist with SPX.

Capital Efficiency

For a $100,000 portfolio running a 2% risk per condor:

Parameter SPX (1 contract) QQQ (15 contracts)
Max loss per contract $1,320 $60
Contracts for $2,000 risk 1 contract ~33 contracts
BPR (Reg-T) $1,500 - $3,000 $2,000 - $6,600
Margin headroom High (BPR is 75-150% of max loss) Lower (BPR is similar to max loss)

For Reg-T accounts, SPX has a margin efficiency advantage because the BPR is similar in absolute dollars to the max loss, while QQQ's BPR scales with the larger contract count.

For portfolio margin accounts, both have similar efficiency. PM calculates BPR based on the largest potential loss in a 1-day or 1-week stress scenario, which is usually a fraction of the max loss.

Diversification: Both

The two underlyings are highly correlated. Running 1 SPX condor and 1 QQQ condor is not meaningful diversification; they will move together during market events. To get real diversification, the condor portfolio should include:

  • 1 SPX condor (broad market exposure)
  • 1 condor on a sector ETF (XLF, XLE, XLV) — different beta drivers
  • 1-2 condors on individual stocks in unrelated sectors

The SPX/QQQ choice is for the broad-market leg of the portfolio. The other legs should be selected for low correlation to SPX.

Choosing Between SPX and QQQ

The decision rule:

  • Default to SPX for margin and taxable accounts. Cash settlement, no assignment risk, deep liquidity, favorable tax treatment.
  • Use QQQ (or another ETF option) for IRAs and retirement accounts, where SPX is not allowed.
  • Use QQQ for smaller accounts where the SPX per-contract risk is too large to size properly. A trader with a $25,000 account cannot risk 2% ($500) on a single SPX contract with $1,320 max loss; the same trader can run 5-8 QQQ contracts with $300-$500 max loss.
  • Use both for diversification in a large portfolio, with the bulk of capital in SPX and a smaller allocation to QQQ for tech exposure.

Most retail condor programs should run 70-80% of capital in SPX and 20-30% in QQQ or other ETF options. The split depends on account type, capital base, and whether the trader wants tech-specific exposure.
