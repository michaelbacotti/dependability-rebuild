Calendar Spread IV Crush Math: Why the Near-Term Option Collapses Faster

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The calendar spread is a bet on the differential between near-term and longer-dated implied volatility. The position profits when IV collapses after a known event (earnings, FDA decision, merger) and the near-term option's IV falls faster than the longer-dated option's IV. The "why" of this differential is not obvious to new calendar traders; the "how" of capturing it as a defined-risk trade is the calendar spread itself.

This article covers the vega-weighted decay differential, the concrete math of an earnings trade, the breakeven analysis, and the practical conditions that make the calendar spread work.

The Core Insight

Before a known event, implied volatility rises. The market prices in the uncertainty about the event outcome. After the event, the uncertainty is resolved, and IV collapses. This is the IV crush.

The IV crush is larger for near-term options than for longer-dated options. The reason: the near-term option's value is dominated by the event, while the longer-dated option's value is dominated by the time remaining after the event.

Component Near-Term Option Longer-Dated Option
Pre-event IV 80-120% (high) 50-70% (also elevated but less)
Post-event IV 30-50% (collapsed) 40-55% (partially retained)
IV change -50 to -70 percentage points -10 to -15 percentage points
Vega High (more sensitive to IV changes) Lower (less sensitive)

The near-term option loses more IV in absolute terms, and it has higher vega, so the dollar value of the IV loss is much larger for the near-term option. The calendar spread is short the near-term option (high vega, big IV loss) and long the longer-dated option (lower vega, smaller IV loss). The differential is the source of profit.

The Vega-Weighted Decay

For a calendar spread on a stock at $150 with earnings in 14 days:

  • Sell the $150 call expiring in 14 days for $5.00 (near-term)
  • Buy the $150 call expiring in 45 days for $8.00 (longer-dated)
  • Net debit: $3.00

The near-term option has a vega of roughly 0.05 per IV point. The longer-dated option has a vega of roughly 0.08 per IV point (the longer-dated option is more sensitive to IV because it has more time value).

Wait — this is the opposite of what we want. The longer-dated option has higher vega, which means a 1-point IV decline costs the long leg more than it costs the short leg.

The reconciliation: the near-term option's IV falls by more in absolute terms (e.g., from 100% to 40%, a 60-point drop), while the longer-dated option's IV falls by less (e.g., from 60% to 45%, a 15-point drop). The dollar impact is:

  • Near-term leg: 60 IV points × 0.05 vega = $3.00 of value loss
  • Longer-dated leg: 15 IV points × 0.08 vega = $1.20 of value loss
  • Differential: $1.80 profit per spread (before transaction costs)

The near-term leg loses $3.00 in value from the IV crush; the longer-dated leg loses $1.20. The trader profits by $1.80 per spread, which is the differential between the two IV declines weighted by vega.

The Concrete Example

A stock at $150 has earnings in 14 days. Pre-earnings IV is 90% for the front-month and 60% for the back-month. Post-earnings IV falls to 40% for the front-month and 50% for the back-month.

Leg Pre-Earnings Post-Earnings Change
Short $150 call (14 DTE) Sell for $5.00 (90% IV) Buy back for $2.00 (40% IV) +$3.00 profit on the short leg
Long $150 call (45 DTE) Buy for $8.00 (60% IV) Sell for $6.80 (50% IV) -$1.20 loss on the long leg
Net position Debit $3.00 Net value $4.80 +$1.80 profit

The position profits $1.80 per spread from the IV crush, before any move in the underlying. This is the "pure IV crush" profit.

The Move Component

The IV crush is only one part of the calendar spread's profit. The other part is the move (or lack of move) in the underlying. The calendar spread is most profitable when the stock stays near the strike after the event. The reason: the near-term option expires worthless (or with low intrinsic value), and the longer-dated option retains its time value.

Stock Move Calendar Spread P&L
Stock stays at $150 (no move) Maximum profit. Near-term expires worthless. Longer-dated retains time value.
Stock moves to $155 (modest move) Smaller profit. Near-term has $5 of intrinsic. Longer-dated has more value but less premium.
Stock moves to $160 (large move) Loss. Near-term has $10 of intrinsic. Longer-dated has more value but the spread is not wide enough.
Stock moves to $145 (modest down) Smaller profit. Near-term expires worthless. Longer-dated has less time value.
Stock moves to $140 (large down) Loss. Near-term expires worthless. Longer-dated loses value.

The "sweet spot" for the calendar spread is a small move in either direction. The position profits from the IV crush and from the time decay differential, but it is damaged by a large move in either direction.

The Breakeven Analysis

The breakeven of a calendar spread is not a single price — it is a range. The position is profitable as long as the underlying is within a range around the strike at expiration of the near-term option.

For a $150/$150 calendar spread with a $3.00 debit:

  • Upper breakeven: roughly $155 (stock at or above this level, the position is at max loss on the upside)
  • Lower breakeven: roughly $145 (stock at or below this level, the position is at max loss on the downside)
  • Profit zone: $145 to $155 (10-point range)

The profit zone is wider when the IV crush is larger and narrower when the IV crush is smaller. The trader wants the IV crush to be as large as possible relative to the move in the underlying.

The Move-Adjusted Return

A useful metric: the ratio of IV crush to underlying move. This measures how much of the position's profit comes from IV crush versus how much comes from being correct on the move.

Scenario IV Crush Underlying Move Return Source
Stock flat, large IV crush 60 pts $0 100% IV crush
Stock moves 2%, large IV crush 60 pts $3 80% IV crush, 20% move
Stock moves 5%, large IV crush 60 pts $7.50 60% IV crush, 40% move
Stock moves 10%, large IV crush 60 pts $15 30% IV crush, 70% move
Stock flat, small IV crush 20 pts $0 Small profit, mostly time decay

The trader wants the IV crush to dominate. A large move in the underlying (5%+) shifts the return source from IV crush to directional bet, which is a different trade with a different risk profile.

The Vega Ratio

The vega ratio of a calendar spread is the ratio of the near-term option's vega to the longer-dated option's vega. A higher vega ratio means the position is more sensitive to the near-term option's IV change, which is the desired exposure.

For the example above:

  • Near-term vega: 0.05
  • Longer-dated vega: 0.08
  • Vega ratio: 0.05 / 0.08 = 0.625

A vega ratio below 1.0 means the near-term option is less sensitive to IV than the longer-dated option. This is the desired structure for an IV crush trade.

For an event with a 60-point IV crush on the near-term and a 15-point IV crush on the longer-dated, the dollar impact is:

  • Near-term: 60 × 0.05 = $3.00
  • Longer-dated: 15 × 0.08 = $1.20
  • Net: $1.80 profit

The Time to Event

The time to event affects both the IV crush size and the vega of the options. The optimal time to event is 14-30 days:

  • 7-14 days: IV is already elevated, but the time decay on the near-term option is also accelerating. The position has less time to capture the IV differential.
  • 14-30 days: IV is rising into the event, and the time decay on the near-term option is moderate. This is the optimal window.
  • 30-60 days: IV has more time to rise, but the near-term option is not as elevated. The IV crush is smaller in absolute terms.
  • 60+ days: IV is not as elevated, and the IV crush is too small to be worth the position cost.

A practical rule: enter the calendar spread 14-30 days before the event for maximum IV differential.

The Strike Selection

The strike selection for a calendar spread is at or near the at-the-money strike. The reason: the at-the-money options have the highest vega, and the IV crush is largest for the at-the-money options.

Strike Selection Vega IV Crush Size Best For
At the money (ATM) Highest Largest Pure IV crush trade
Slightly OTM (1-2%) High Large Slightly directional bias
Deep OTM (5%+) Low Small Not appropriate for IV crush
Deep ITM (5%+) High but dominated by intrinsic Small relative to intrinsic Not appropriate for IV crush

The default strike is at-the-money or 1-2% out-of-the-money. The deeper OTM or ITM strikes are not appropriate because their IV crush is too small relative to the position cost.

The Expiration Pairing

The expiration pairing affects the vega ratio and the time decay differential. The standard pairings:

Near-Term DTE Longer-Dated DTE Vega Ratio Time Decay Differential
7-14 days 30-45 days 0.4-0.6 Large
14-30 days 45-75 days 0.5-0.7 Moderate
30-45 days 75-120 days 0.6-0.8 Smaller

A tighter pairing (7-14 days vs 30-45 days) has a higher IV differential per day but a lower vega ratio. A wider pairing (30-45 days vs 75-120 days) has a higher vega ratio but a smaller IV differential per day.

The optimal pairing depends on the event. For earnings (a single-day event), a 14-30 day pairing is standard. For a longer-term catalyst (merger close, FDA decision within 60 days), a 30-45 day pairing may be appropriate.
