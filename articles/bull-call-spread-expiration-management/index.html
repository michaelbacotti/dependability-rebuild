Bull Call Spread Expiration Management: When to Close, the 2-Week Rule, and Rolling

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

A bull call spread is entered with a directional view and a target. The position can be held to expiration, closed at a profit target, or closed at a stop. The exit decision is as important as the entry, and a well-entered spread can still be a losing trade if the exit is poorly timed. The most common exit mistakes are holding too long (letting time decay erase the gains), closing too early (taking profit at a small fraction of the max), and rolling the position into a worse setup.

The Three Exit Paths

A bull call spread has three possible exits:

  1. Profit target hit: Close the spread for a profit. The standard target is 50-100% of the debit paid.
  2. Stop loss triggered: Close the spread for a loss. The standard stop is 50-100% of the debit paid.
  3. Expiration: Hold the spread to expiration. The position settles at the spread value at expiration, which is the intrinsic value of the long call minus the intrinsic value of the short call.

The profit target and stop loss are mechanical exits that remove the discretionary element from the trade. The expiration exit is appropriate when the trader has a strong view that the position will be at max profit at expiration.

The 2-Week Rule

The single most important exit rule: close the position 2 weeks (10 trading days) before expiration, regardless of profit or loss.

The reasoning: in the final 2 weeks, the position is dominated by gamma and time decay. The time value of the long and short calls is small, and the position's value is essentially the intrinsic value. A small move in the underlying in the final 2 weeks can produce a large mark-to-market change.

For a position that is profitable at 14 DTE, closing locks in the profit. For a position that is unprofitable at 14 DTE, closing limits the loss. The 2-week rule is not about predicting the move; it is about removing the gamma risk from the position.

A bull call spread is a defined-risk position, so the max loss is the debit paid. But the 2-week rule is about realized loss, not max loss. A position that is unprofitable at 14 DTE and held to expiration can lose the full debit. Closing at 14 DTE for 50% of the debit caps the realized loss at 50% of the max loss.

Profit Target Rules

Standard profit targets for bull call spreads:

Target When to Use Notes
50% of debit Conservative Locks in profit early; reduces exposure to a reversal
75% of debit Standard Balanced between capturing profit and giving the trade room to work
100% of debit (double) Aggressive Waits for the position to double; common in earnings trades
100%+ of max profit Maximum capture Holds for full max profit; requires the stock to reach the short strike

A 50% profit target is reached when the spread's value has increased by 50% of the debit paid. For a $2.50 debit, the 50% target is reached when the spread is worth $3.75 (50% gain on the debit).

The 50% target is appropriate for moderate-conviction trades where the trader wants to lock in a meaningful gain and move on to the next setup. The 100% target is appropriate for high-conviction trades where the trader has a strong view on the move and wants to maximize the gain.

A useful rule: use 50% as the default and 100% for trades where the stock is at or above the short strike with 21+ DTE remaining.

Stop Loss Rules

Standard stop loss for bull call spreads:

Stop When to Use Notes
50% of debit Standard Closes at 50% loss; common in swing trades
75% of debit Aggressive Gives the position more room; appropriate for volatile underlyings
100% of debit Maximum Holds to expiration; only appropriate for high-conviction trades

A 50% stop is the most common. The position is closed if it loses 50% of the debit paid. For a $2.50 debit, the 50% stop is triggered when the spread is worth $1.25 (50% loss on the debit).

The 50% stop gives the position room to be wrong initially and still recover. A tighter stop (25% of debit) produces more whipsaws in volatile markets; a wider stop (75% of debit) gives the position more room but increases the realized loss when the stop is triggered.

Holding to Expiration

Holding a bull call spread to expiration is appropriate when:

  • The position is at or near max profit and the trader wants to capture the full value
  • The position is at or near max loss and the trader does not want to realize the loss before expiration
  • The underlying is between the long and short strikes, and the trader wants to capture the intrinsic value at settlement

For a position held to expiration with the stock at $108 (long strike $102, short strike $110), the spread settles at $6.00 ($108 - $102 long intrinsic, $110 short OTM). The trader captures the full $6.00 minus the debit paid.

For a position held to expiration with the stock at $101 (below the long strike), the spread settles at $0. Both options expire worthless, and the trader loses the full debit.

The 2-week rule supersedes the hold-to-expiration logic. If the position is at 14 DTE and not at max profit, the gamma risk in the final 2 weeks makes holding more dangerous than closing.

Rolling the Position Forward

A bull call spread that needs more time can be rolled forward — close the current spread and open a new spread at a later expiration. The roll can be at the same strikes or at different strikes.

Same-strike roll: Close the current spread for a debit (or take the loss), then open a new spread at the same strikes for a later expiration. The cost is the difference in debit between the two expirations. The new spread has more time to work.

Strike-change roll: Close the current spread and open a new spread at different strikes. The cost is the net of the close and the new open. The new spread has a different breakeven and max profit.

A roll is appropriate when:

  • The position is in profit but the trader wants to give it more time to reach max profit
  • The position is at a small loss and the trader has a higher-conviction view on a longer timeframe
  • The original thesis still holds but the timing was off

A roll is not appropriate when:

  • The original thesis is broken (the stock has moved significantly against the position)
  • The cost of the roll exceeds 50% of the original debit
  • The underlying is below the long strike and the position is likely to expire worthless

The Time Decay Curve

Time decay (theta) is not linear. For a bull call spread, theta is highest in the final 30 days before expiration. The position loses the most to time decay in the last month.

DTE Daily Theta (per spread) Cumulative Loss to Theta
60 -0.02 $0 (entry)
45 -0.03 $0.45
30 -0.05 $1.20
21 -0.06 $1.80
14 -0.07 $2.50
7 -0.10 $3.50
0 -0.00 $4.00

The position loses roughly 60% of its time value in the final 30 days. This is why the 2-week rule is so important: the final 2 weeks are when the position is hemorrhaging time value.

The Profit Decay Curve

For a position that is profitable, the time decay is not all bad. The position's value increases as the underlying moves up, and the time decay reduces the gain. The net effect depends on the underlying's move.

For a position that is unprofitable, the time decay accelerates the loss. The position's value decreases as the underlying does not move, and the time decay adds to the loss.

A useful heuristic: a profitable position at 21 DTE should usually be closed (the 50% target is reached). An unprofitable position at 21 DTE should usually be closed (the 2-week rule applies). The 21 DTE mark is the decision point.

The 21 DTE Decision Tree

At 21 DTE, the position falls into one of three categories:

  1. Profit > 50% of debit: Close. Take the profit. The 50% target has been reached.
  2. Loss < 50% of debit: Hold. The position has room to work, and the underlying is within the spread.
  3. Loss > 50% of debit: Close. The position is unlikely to recover, and the time decay in the final 3 weeks will erase any remaining value.

This decision tree is mechanical and removes the discretionary element from the exit. The trader who follows it consistently will have a higher win rate and a lower average loss than a trader who makes discretionary exit decisions.

Common Exit Mistakes

Holding for max profit when the target is reached. The 50% target is reached at some point before the stock reaches the short strike. Closing at 50% locks in a meaningful gain. Waiting for max profit exposes the position to a reversal in the final weeks.

Closing at a loss too early. A position that is -25% of debit is within the normal range of a bull call spread. Closing at -25% is a small loss, but the position may have recovered to a gain if given more time. The 50% stop gives the position room to work.

Rolling a losing position. A position that is -75% of debit is not a candidate for rolling. The cost of the roll is high, the original thesis is likely broken, and the roll extends the loss. Close the position and move on.

Failing to adjust for events. A bull call spread with a major event (earnings, FOMC) within 5 trading days is exposed to a large gap. The position should be closed before the event, regardless of profit or loss. The event-day move can produce a large mark-to-market change that overwhelms the position's defined risk.

Putting It Together

The exit framework:

  1. At entry: Set the profit target (50% of debit) and the stop loss (50% of debit).
  2. At 21 DTE: Apply the decision tree. Close at 50%+ profit, close at 50%+ loss, hold otherwise.
  3. At 14 DTE: Close the position regardless of profit or loss (the 2-week rule).
  4. At any time: Close the position if the underlying moves significantly against the thesis or if a major event is within 5 days.

The framework is mechanical. The trader who follows it removes the emotional element from the exit decision and produces a more consistent return.
