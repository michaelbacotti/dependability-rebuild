Cash-Secured Put Reserve Cash Sizing: How Much Cash to Set Aside

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

A cash-secured put (CSP) requires holding enough cash to buy the underlying stock if the put is assigned. The amount of cash to reserve is the strike price multiplied by 100 (for stocks) or the strike price multiplied by the contract multiplier (for SPX). The sizing of the reserved cash is the central capital allocation question for CSPs, and the answer depends on the strike selection, the position size, the portfolio's other holdings, and the trader's view on the underlying.

The Basic Reserve Calculation

For a cash-secured put, the cash reserve is:

Cash Reserve = Strike Price × 100 (for stocks) × Number of Contracts

For a put sold at a $95 strike on a $100 stock, with 1 contract:

Cash Reserve = $95 × 100 × 1 = $9,500

The reserved cash is held in the account, earning interest (in a money market fund or similar) but not available for other trades. The opportunity cost is the interest that the cash would have earned in a higher-yielding investment.

The Strike Selection Impact

The strike selection determines the cash reserve. A higher strike (closer to the current price) requires more cash; a lower strike (further from the current price) requires less cash.

Strike Current Stock Price Cash Reserve per Contract Premium
$100 (ATM) $100 $10,000 $3.00
$95 (5% OTM) $100 $9,500 $1.50
$90 (10% OTM) $100 $9,000 $0.75
$85 (15% OTM) $100 $8,500 $0.30

The trade-off: a higher strike (ATM) collects more premium but requires more cash and is more likely to be assigned. A lower strike (15% OTM) requires less cash and is less likely to be assigned but collects less premium.

A useful rule: choose the strike that balances premium against cash reserve and assignment probability. The 5-10% OTM strikes are usually the best balance for income generation.

The Position Sizing Rules

The position sizing for CSPs follows the same 2% rule as other defined-risk strategies:

Max risk per CSP = 2% of portfolio capital

For a $100,000 portfolio:

Max risk per CSP = $100,000 × 0.02 = $2,000

The "max risk" for a CSP is typically the difference between the strike and the expected assignment price, adjusted for the premium received. For a $95 strike with $1.50 premium on a $100 stock:

Component Value
Strike $95
Premium received $1.50
Effective cost basis if assigned $93.50
Current stock price $100
Loss vs current price if assigned $6.50

The "loss vs current price if assigned" is the max risk per contract. For a $2,000 risk budget, the position can be 3 contracts ($19.50 loss × 3 = $58.50... wait, that's not right. Let me recalculate.)

Actually, the max risk per CSP is more nuanced. The position is fully cash-secured, so the max loss is the difference between the cash reserve and the stock's value at assignment, minus the premium received. For a $95 strike with $1.50 premium:

Component Value
Cash reserve $9,500
Stock value if assigned (at $95 strike) $9,500
Premium received $150
Net cost if assigned $9,350
Loss if stock goes to $0 $9,350

The max loss for a CSP is the full cash reserve minus the premium received. The position is essentially equivalent to owning the stock at a $1.50 discount, with the obligation to buy at the strike.

For position sizing, the 2% rule is applied to the max loss, which is the cash reserve minus the premium. For a $9,500 cash reserve with $1.50 premium:

Max risk per contract = $9,500 - $150 = $9,350

For a $100,000 portfolio with 2% risk ($2,000), the position can be 0 contracts (since $9,350 > $2,000). The CSP is too large for a $100,000 portfolio at the 2% rule.

The 2% rule for CSPs is therefore applied differently. The rule is often:

Max cash reserve per CSP = 2% of portfolio capital

For a $100,000 portfolio:

Max cash reserve per CSP = $100,000 × 0.02 = $2,000

For a $9,500 cash reserve per contract, the position can be 0 contracts at the 2% rule (since $9,500 > $2,000).

This is the fundamental tension of CSPs: the cash reserve is large, and the 2% rule limits the position to a small number of contracts. The CSP is a capital-intensive strategy.

The Smaller Strike Solution

For smaller portfolios, the strike can be lowered to fit the 2% rule. A $20 strike requires $2,000 cash reserve, which fits the 2% rule for a $100,000 portfolio.

Strike Cash Reserve per Contract Position Size at 2% Rule
$100 $10,000 0 contracts
$50 $5,000 0 contracts
$20 $2,000 1 contract
$10 $1,000 2 contracts
$5 $500 4 contracts

The smaller-strike solution allows position sizing, but the premium collected is also smaller. A $5 strike may collect only $0.10-$0.20 in premium, which is a 2-4% return on the cash reserve for a 30-day put. The return on cash is reasonable, but the absolute premium is small.

The Multi-Contract Solution

For larger portfolios, the multi-contract solution allows multiple contracts at the same strike. A $500,000 portfolio with 2% risk ($10,000) can run 1 contract at a $10,000 cash reserve, 5 contracts at a $2,000 cash reserve, or 2 contracts at a $5,000 cash reserve.

Portfolio Size Strike Cash Reserve per Contract Contracts at 2% Rule
$100,000 $100 $10,000 0
$100,000 $20 $2,000 1
$500,000 $100 $10,000 1
$500,000 $50 $5,000 2
$1,000,000 $100 $10,000 2

The multi-contract solution is appropriate for larger portfolios. The trader with $500K+ can run 1-2 contracts at standard strikes, which produces meaningful premium income.

The Opportunity Cost of Reserved Cash

The reserved cash earns interest but is not invested in higher-yielding assets. The opportunity cost is the difference between the cash yield and the yield of the next-best investment.

For a $9,500 cash reserve earning 4.5% (current money market rate):

Time Period Interest Earned Opportunity Cost vs 5% Treasury
30 days $35.62 $3.42 (0.5% × $9,500 × 30/365)
90 days $106.85 $10.27
365 days $433.50 $41.67

The opportunity cost is small in absolute terms but meaningful relative to the premium collected. A $1.50 premium on a $9,500 cash reserve over 30 days is a 1.5% return on cash. The opportunity cost of 0.5% (vs a Treasury) reduces the net return to 1.0%.

The Naked Put Alternative

A naked put (sold on margin) does not require cash reserve. The broker holds margin based on the maximum potential loss, but the cash is not reserved. The position is more capital-efficient but has a different risk profile.

Parameter Cash-Secured Put Naked Put (Margin)
Cash reserve $9,500 (full strike) $2,000-$3,000 (margin)
Premium collected $1.50 $1.50
Max loss $9,350 (full cash reserve) $9,350 (same)
Buying power efficiency Low High
Liquidity risk None (cash is available) Margin call risk if position moves against

The naked put is more capital-efficient but has higher liquidity risk. The trader who sells a naked put on margin and the position moves against them may face a margin call, which forces them to deposit additional cash or close the position at a loss.

The CSP is the conservative version; the naked put is the leveraged version. The choice depends on the trader's risk tolerance and capital base.

The Account Type Considerations

The CSP is appropriate for accounts that require cash-secured positions:

  • IRA accounts: Most IRA accounts cannot hold naked options. The CSP is the only short-put strategy available.
  • Cash accounts: Cash accounts require full payment for securities purchases. The CSP is the appropriate short-put strategy.
  • Margin accounts with conservative risk tolerance: The trader who wants the safety of cash-secured positions can use the CSP in a margin account.

For margin accounts with higher risk tolerance, the naked put is a viable alternative. The choice depends on the trader's preferences.

The Strike Selection for Income

The strike selection for income-focused CSPs typically favors higher strikes (closer to ATM) because they collect more premium. The trade-off is the higher assignment probability.

Strike Premium Assignment Probability (30 DTE) Return on Cash
ATM (100%) 3.0% of strike 50% 3.0% × 50% = 1.5% expected
5% OTM (95%) 1.5% of strike 30% 1.5% × 30% = 0.45% expected
10% OTM (90%) 0.75% of strike 15% 0.75% × 15% = 0.11% expected
15% OTM (85%) 0.30% of strike 7% 0.30% × 7% = 0.02% expected

The expected return on cash (premium × probability) is highest for ATM strikes. The ATM CSP has the highest expected return but also the highest assignment probability.

The Strike Selection for Stock Acquisition

For traders who want to acquire the stock at a discount, the strike selection favors lower strikes (further OTM). The premium is smaller, but the assignment is at a more attractive price.

Strike Premium Effective Cost Basis Discount to Current
$100 (ATM) $3.00 $97.00 3.0%
$95 (5% OTM) $1.50 $93.50 6.5%
$90 (10% OTM) $0.75 $89.25 10.75%

The $90 strike produces a 10.75% discount to the current price if assigned. The premium is smaller, but the entry price is more attractive. This is the appropriate strategy for traders who want to buy the stock at a discount and are comfortable waiting for the assignment.

The Decision Rule

A practical algorithm for sizing the cash reserve:

  1. Determine the portfolio size.
  2. Apply the 2% rule: max cash reserve per CSP = 2% of portfolio.
  3. Choose the strike that fits the 2% rule. If the standard strike is too large, use a smaller strike.
  4. Determine the position size based on the cash reserve and the 2% rule.
  5. Consider the opportunity cost of the reserved cash. The expected premium should exceed the opportunity cost by a meaningful margin.
  6. Choose between CSP and naked put based on the account type and risk tolerance.

The decision is capital-driven. The trader who has a $100K portfolio and wants to run a $100 strike CSP must either reduce the position size to fit the 2% rule, use a smaller strike, or use a naked put on margin.

The Multi-Strike Diversification

For a portfolio running multiple CSPs, the strikes should be diversified across underlyings. The cash reserves should be spread across:

  • Different sectors (tech, healthcare, finance, consumer)
  • Different market caps (large, mid, small)
  • Different volatility profiles (high, medium, low)

The diversification reduces the correlation of the cash reserves and the assignment risk. A market event that affects one sector does not affect all sectors.
