Calendar Spread Exit Timing: Close 1-3 Days Post-Event, IV Crush Completion Rules

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

A calendar spread is typically held through a known event (earnings, FDA decision, merger) and closed shortly after. The IV crush completes within 1-3 trading days after the event for most equities, and the calendar spread's profit is concentrated in that window. Holding past the IV crush completion is the single most common calendar spread mistake, because the position is exposed to additional time decay and the move in the underlying without the corresponding IV crush benefit.

The IV Crush Completion Curve

The IV crush does not happen all at once. It begins 1-2 days before the event (as some traders close their positions pre-event) and completes 1-3 days after the event (as the remaining uncertainty is resolved).

Day Typical IV Level (% of pre-event) Cumulative IV Crush
T-2 (2 days before) 95% 5%
T-1 (1 day before) 100% (peak) 0%
T (event day) 60-70% 30-40%
T+1 (1 day after) 45-55% 45-55%
T+2 (2 days after) 40-50% 50-60%
T+3 (3 days after) 40-50% 50-60% (essentially complete)
T+5 (5 days after) 40-50% 50-60% (stable)

The IV crush is 30-40% complete on the event day and 50-60% complete by T+2. The remaining 40-50% of the IV crush is small and happens over the next 2-3 days as the market fully digests the event.

The 1-3 Day Post-Event Window

The standard exit window for a calendar spread is 1-3 trading days after the event. The reasons:

  1. IV crush is mostly complete. By T+2, the IV crush is 50-60% complete. The remaining crush is small and slow.
  2. Time decay continues. The near-term option is still losing time value daily, but the dollar value of the decay is small (because the option has little time value left).
  3. Move risk increases. The longer the position is held after the event, the more exposure to the underlying's move. The directional risk of the calendar spread is at its maximum after the event.
  4. Capital is tied up. The position consumes buying power that could be deployed elsewhere. Holding past T+3 ties up capital for diminishing returns.

A practical rule: close the calendar spread on T+2 (2 trading days after the event) unless the position is at a clear profit target or the trader has a specific view on the post-event move.

The Pre-Event vs Post-Event Exit

Some traders close the calendar spread before the event, capturing the IV rise that has already happened. The pre-event exit is appropriate when:

  • The IV has risen to the expected level and the trader wants to lock in the gain
  • The trader is concerned about the event-day gap risk
  • The trader has used the calendar as a vega play and wants to exit the vega exposure

The post-event exit (the default) is appropriate when:

  • The trader wants to capture the IV crush itself
  • The IV has not fully risen yet (the position is entered before the IV peak)
  • The trader is comfortable with the event-day gap risk

A useful comparison:

Exit Timing IV Captured Move Risk Capital Tied Up
Pre-event (T-1) 80-90% of full crush None 1-2 days less
Post-event (T+2) 100% of full crush Moderate (2 days) Standard
Post-event (T+5) 105-110% of full crush High (5 days) 3-4 days more

The pre-event exit captures most of the IV crush and avoids the event-day risk. The post-event exit captures the full IV crush and accepts the event-day risk.

The IV Crush Completion Indicator

A practical indicator for the IV crush completion: track the IV of the near-term option daily after the event. When the IV stops declining (or declines by less than 1 point per day), the crush is essentially complete.

Day Near-Term IV Daily Change Crush Status
T-1 90% +2 pts Pre-peak
T 65% -25 pts Major crush
T+1 50% -15 pts Continued crush
T+2 45% -5 pts Crush slowing
T+3 43% -2 pts Crush nearly complete
T+5 42% -1 pt Crush complete

When the daily IV change drops below 2 points, the crush is essentially complete. The trader can close the position with confidence that the remaining IV crush will be small.

The Move-Adjusted Exit

The calendar spread's exit is not solely based on time. The position's value also depends on the underlying's move. The trader should close the position when:

  1. The IV crush is complete (T+2 or T+3), OR
  2. The position has reached a profit target (e.g., 100% of debit), OR
  3. The position has hit a stop loss (e.g., 50% of debit), OR
  4. The underlying has moved beyond the breakeven range

The earliest of these conditions triggers the exit.

Exit Condition Typical Timing Action
T+2 reached, position in profit T+2 Close at market
Profit target reached (100% of debit) Anytime Close immediately
Stop loss triggered (50% of debit) Anytime Close immediately
Underlying outside breakeven range Anytime Close immediately

The Profit Target Rules

Calendar spread profit targets are different from directional spread targets. The position is not held for max profit (which is capped at the spread width minus the debit); it is held for the IV crush.

Target When to Use Notes
50% of debit Conservative Lock in a small gain early; common for low-IV events
100% of debit (2x) Standard Capture a meaningful gain; common for earnings events
200% of debit (3x) Aggressive Capture a large gain; requires a small underlying move and large IV crush
300% of debit (4x) Maximum Rare; requires a perfect setup (large IV crush + small move)

The 100% target is the most common. The position is closed when the spread's value reaches 2x the debit paid.

For a $3.00 debit calendar spread:

  • 50% target: spread value of $4.50 (50% gain)
  • 100% target: spread value of $6.00 (100% gain)
  • 200% target: spread value of $9.00 (200% gain)
  • 300% target: spread value of $12.00 (300% gain)

The 100% target is reached in the typical earnings trade. The 200% and 300% targets require a combination of large IV crush and small move, which is rare.

The Stop Loss Rules

Calendar spread stop losses are critical because the position can lose more than expected if the underlying moves sharply.

Stop When to Use Notes
25% of debit Tight Common for small accounts; produces more whipsaws
50% of debit Standard Balanced; appropriate for most accounts
75% of debit Wide Gives the position room; appropriate for high-vol underlyings
100% of debit Maximum Holds to expiration; only for high-conviction trades

The 50% stop is the most common. The position is closed if it loses 50% of the debit paid. For a $3.00 debit calendar spread, the 50% stop is triggered when the spread is worth $1.50 (50% loss on the debit).

The Underlying's Move Impact

The move in the underlying affects the calendar spread's value. A large move in either direction can damage the position.

Underlying Move Calendar Spread Impact
0-2% Positive or neutral; position captures IV crush
2-5% Mixed; position may profit or lose depending on direction
5-10% Negative; position is likely at a loss
10%+ Significantly negative; position is likely at max loss

The 5% move is the typical threshold beyond which the calendar spread becomes a losing trade regardless of the IV crush. The trader who holds through a 5%+ move has converted an IV crush trade into a directional bet, which is a different trade with a different risk profile.

The Decision Rule for Exit

A practical algorithm for exiting a calendar spread:

  1. Is the position at a profit target (100% of debit)? Close.
  2. Is the position at a stop loss (50% of debit)? Close.
  3. Is the underlying outside the breakeven range (typically 5% from the strike)? Close.
  4. Has the IV crush completed (T+2 or T+3)? Close.
  5. Is the time to expiration of the near-term option less than 3 days? Close.

The earliest of these conditions triggers the exit. The trader who follows the rule closes the position within 1-3 days after the event, capturing most of the IV crush and avoiding the directional risk.

The Common Mistakes

Holding past T+3. The IV crush is essentially complete by T+2 or T+3. Holding past that point ties up capital for diminishing returns and increases the directional risk.

Closing too early (T+0 or T+1). The IV crush is only 30-50% complete on T+0 to T+1. Closing too early leaves profit on the table. The standard exit is T+2.

Holding through a 5%+ move. A 5%+ move in the underlying damages the calendar spread regardless of the IV crush. The trader who holds through a 5%+ move has converted the trade into a directional bet.

Not setting a stop loss. Calendar spreads can lose 100% of the debit if the underlying moves sharply. The trader who does not set a stop loss is exposed to a full loss on the position.

Failing to track the IV. The IV crush is the source of profit for the calendar spread. The trader who does not track the IV daily cannot identify the crush completion and may hold the position past the optimal exit point.

The Post-Exit Trade

After closing the calendar spread, the trader has capital and can enter a new position. The post-exit trade depends on the trader's view:

  • If the underlying has moved: Enter a directional trade in the direction of the move (debit spread or long call/put).
  • If the underlying has not moved: Enter a new calendar spread for the next event (e.g., next quarter's earnings) or a different strategy (iron condor, credit spread).

The capital freed up from the calendar spread exit can be deployed in the highest-conviction trade available.
