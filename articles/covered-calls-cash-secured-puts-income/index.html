Covered Calls and Cash-Secured Puts: Complete Income Strategy Guide

May 31, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The covered call and the cash-secured put are the two foundational income strategies in options trading. Both collect premium in exchange for an obligation — the difference is whether you're selling upside (covered call) or downside protection (cash-secured put). Understanding when to use each, and how to manage the position when things go wrong, is what separates consistent income generation from simply hoping your stock stays flat.

What Is a Covered Call?

A covered call involves owning 100 shares of an underlying stock and selling one call option per 100 shares. The call is typically out-of-the-money — above the current stock price — and generates income while you hold the shares.

Why sell the call?

  • You collect premium upfront for accepting the obligation to sell your shares at the strike price
  • If the stock stays below the strike, the call expires worthless and you keep the premium
  • If the stock rises above the strike, you're obligated to sell — but at a price you agreed to in advance

The income math:

  • Stock at $100, you own 100 shares
  • Sell a 30-day $105 call for $2.00 premium
  • If stock stays below $105 at expiration: call expires worthless, you keep $2.00 = $200 income on $10,000 of stock exposure = 2% monthly return
  • If stock rises above $105 and is assigned: you sell at $105, losing upside above $105, but you collected $2.00 upfront

Key constraint: You must own the shares. This limits your risk on the short call — you're covered. But it also caps your upside at the strike price.

What Is a Cash-Secured Put?

A cash-secured put involves selling a put option while holding enough cash to buy the underlying stock if assigned. You pick a strike at or below your target buy-in price, collect premium, and wait.

Why sell the put?

  • You collect premium for accepting the obligation to buy the stock at the strike
  • If the stock stays above the strike, the put expires worthless and you keep the premium
  • If the stock drops below the strike and is assigned, you buy at a price you predetermined — below the current market price

The income math:

  • Stock at $100, you want to buy at $95
  • Sell a 30-day $95 put for $1.50 premium
  • If stock stays above $95 at expiration: put expires worthless, you keep $1.50 = $150 income
  • If stock drops below $95 and is assigned: you buy at $95 (below current $100), premium offsets cost basis

Key constraint: You must hold enough cash to cover the purchase. Hence "cash-secured." This isn't a margin strategy — you're using cash you've set aside to back the obligation.

Covered Call vs Cash-Secured Put — Which to Use

| Factor | Covered Call | Cash-Secured Put |

|---|---|---|

| Requires owning stock | Yes | No (requires cash instead) |

| Best when | Stock is near your target sell price | Stock is near your target buy price |

| Upside potential | Capped at strike | Unlimited (stock can go higher) |

| Downside risk | Stock can drop to zero | Limited to difference between strike and stock |

| Income source | Selling upside | Selling downside protection |

| Assignment risk | If stock rises above strike | If stock drops below strike |

| Ex-dividend concern | Must manage before ex-date | Less relevant |

Rule of thumb: Sell a covered call when you own a stock and want to generate income while setting a price at which you're willing to sell. Sell a cash-secured put when you want to buy a stock at a lower price and earn income while waiting for that opportunity.

When to Use Covered Calls

Ideal conditions:

  • You own a stock with a modest price target and want to earn income while waiting
  • You don't expect the stock to make a large move higher in the near term
  • You've already achieved a gain and want to generate income on shares you'd be willing to sell
  • You want to partially hedge a long stock position with premium income

The strike selection process:

  1. Identify your price target — where would you sell the stock today?
  2. Choose a strike slightly above your target (1-5% above current price)
  3. Select expiration 3-6 weeks out — enough time to collect meaningful premium, not so long that you give up too much upside
  4. Collect at least $0.50-$1.00 per spread — below this threshold, transaction costs and management overhead outweigh the income

Sizing and position management:

  • One call per 100 shares (standard contract)
  • Don't sell calls on more shares than you own — that creates a naked short and unlimited risk
  • If holding multiple lots, consider which lot you're protecting

When to Use Cash-Secured Puts

Ideal conditions:

  • You want to buy a stock at a lower price than current trading levels
  • You have cash set aside and are willing to own the stock at your target price
  • The stock has been range-bound and you're comfortable waiting for an entry
  • You want to generate income while waiting for a buy-in opportunity

The strike selection process:

  1. Identify your target buy-in price — where would you buy if the stock dropped?
  2. Choose a strike at or slightly below your target (at-the-money or slightly out-of-the-money)
  3. Select expiration 3-6 weeks out for the same reason as covered calls
  4. Size the position: if assigned, can you buy 100 shares at the strike with your reserved cash?

Avoid:

  • Selling puts on stocks you'd never want to own
  • Selling puts with strikes far below a price you'd realistically buy (you're setting up for a bad assignment)
  • Overcommitting: if all your cash is tied up in put obligations, you can't act when opportunity appears

Exit Rules — Covered Call

Buy back the call (close the position) when:

  • Stock drops >10-15% from your cost basis — the premium you collected doesn't compensate for a significant decline
  • The call is deep in-the-money with little time left and you want to avoid assignment
  • You change your bullish thesis and want to sell the shares

Roll the call when:

  • Stock runs up near the short strike and you want to stay invested — roll up (higher strike) and out (later expiration) to collect more credit
  • You collect more premium on the roll, extending the position's runway

Assignment management:

  • If you're assigned (stock closed above strike at expiration): you sell the shares at the strike
  • You still kept the premium, so your effective sell price = strike + premium received
  • After assignment, you can immediately sell another call (at a higher strike) on shares you no longer own — but this would be a naked call, so don't do it without a plan

Exit Rules — Cash-Secured Put

Roll the put when:

  • Stock drops below the short strike and you want to maintain the position — roll down (lower strike) and out (later expiration)
  • Rolling collects additional credit and gives the stock more time to recover

Assignment management:

  • If assigned: you buy the stock at the strike price
  • Your net cost = strike price - premium received
  • Example: $100 stock, sold $95 put for $1.50 → assigned at $95 → net cost basis = $93.50
  • After assignment, you can sell a covered call on the new shares to generate income

Buy back the put when:

  • Stock has dropped significantly and you want to cut the loss (close the put before it becomes deep ITM)
  • The put has appreciated significantly and closing is cheaper than holding to expiration
  • You change your thesis and no longer want the obligation to buy

Common Mistakes

1. Selling calls on stocks you don't want to sell

The covered call obligates you to sell at the strike. If you're bullish and don't want to sell, don't sell the call — or sell a strike far above your target so assignment is unlikely.

Fix: Only sell covered calls on shares you'd be comfortable selling at the strike price today.

2. Not managing ex-dividend dates

If you're holding a covered call and the stock goes ex-dividend, assignment risk increases. A call holder who wants the dividend may exercise just before ex-date.

Fix: Close or roll covered calls before ex-dividend dates if you don't want to risk early assignment.

3. Selling puts without reserved cash

Selling a cash-secured put means you're obligated to buy at the strike. If you don't have the cash, you're using margin — which defeats the purpose of "cash-secured."

Fix: Only sell puts on positions you can afford to be assigned. If assigned at a $50 strike on 100 shares, you need $5,000 in cash reserves.

4. Not rolling when able

Both covered calls and cash-secured puts can be rolled — buying back the short option and selling a new one at a different strike and/or expiration. Rolling extends the position and collects additional credit.

Fix: When a position moves against you (stock drops for covered call / put, or rises for call), evaluate rolling before simply holding to expiration.

5. Capita allocation too concentrated

Selling 10 covered calls on 10 different stocks with no cash reserves is a recipe for margin calls if the market drops.

Fix: Treat each covered call or cash-secured put as a defined risk position. Reserve cash for put assignments, don't overcommit on covered calls in a bull market that could reverse.

Relationship Between Covered Calls and Cash-Secured Puts

Many experienced income traders use these strategies in combination:

Example — the wheel strategy:

  1. Sell a cash-secured put at your target buy price
  2. If assigned: you buy the stock at the strike (below market)
  3. Now own the stock — sell a covered call at your target sell price
  4. If called away: you sell at the strike (above market), premium collected
  5. Repeat: sell another put at the new target buy price

The wheel generates income through both sides — put premium when waiting to buy, call premium when holding stock you've already bought.
