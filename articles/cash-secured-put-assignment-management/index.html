Cash-Secured Put Assignment Management: What Happens and Cost Basis Calculation

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

A cash-secured put (CSP) can be assigned if the underlying stock drops below the strike at expiration (or in some cases, before expiration). The assignment means the trader is obligated to buy 100 shares of the stock at the strike price. For the trader who entered the CSP as a way to acquire the stock at a discount, the assignment is a feature. For the trader who entered the CSP purely for income, the assignment is a complication that requires management.

The Assignment Mechanism

When a put option is assigned, the option holder exercises the right to sell the shares at the strike. For the CSP seller, the assignment means:

  1. The broker debits the cash reserve from the account (or converts the existing cash to cover the purchase).
  2. The broker credits 100 shares of the underlying stock to the account.
  3. The put option is removed from the account (it has been exercised).

The assignment happens automatically at expiration if the put is in the money (ITM). For American-style options (which includes all stock options), the assignment can also happen before expiration if the option holder exercises early.

The Effective Cost Basis

The effective cost basis is the price the trader has effectively paid for the shares, after accounting for the premium received.

Effective Cost Basis = Strike Price - Premium Received

For a $95 strike put with $1.50 premium:

Effective Cost Basis = $95 - $1.50 = $93.50 per share

For a full contract (100 shares):

Effective Cost Basis = $9,500 - $150 = $9,350 per contract

The effective cost basis is $1.50 below the strike, which is the premium received. The trader has effectively bought the stock at a $1.50 discount to the strike.

The Cost Basis with Multiple Cycles

For a wheel trader who has sold multiple puts before assignment, the effective cost basis includes all premiums received:

Premium Strike Cumulative Premium Effective Cost Basis
1st put $95 $1.50 $93.50
2nd put (after 1st expired worthless) $95 $3.00 $92.00
3rd put (after 2nd expired worthless) $95 $4.50 $90.50
4th put (assigned) $95 $6.00 $89.00

The cumulative premium is $6.00 (4 puts × $1.50), and the effective cost basis is $95 - $6.00 = $89.00. The wheel trader has acquired the stock at a $6.00 discount to the strike.

The Tax Treatment of Assignment

The CSP assignment has specific tax implications. The premium received from the put is treated as a short-term capital gain (if the put was held for less than a year) or a long-term capital gain (if held for more than a year). The stock purchase at the strike is a new position with a cost basis equal to the strike minus the premium.

For tax purposes:

Event Tax Treatment
Put premium received Short-term or long-term capital gain (based on holding period)
Stock purchase at assignment New long-term or short-term position with cost basis = strike - premium
Future sale of stock Capital gain or loss based on the cost basis

The put premium is taxed in the year it is received, regardless of whether the put expires worthless or is assigned. The stock's cost basis is the strike minus the premium, and the future sale of the stock is taxed based on this cost basis.

The Post-Assignment Decisions

After the put is assigned, the trader has 100 shares of the underlying stock. The trader must decide what to do with the shares. The three main options:

  1. Hold the shares: Treat the shares as a long-term investment. The CSP has effectively become a stock purchase at a discount.
  1. Sell a covered call: Generate additional income on the newly acquired shares. The covered call is the natural complement to the CSP, and the combination is the wheel strategy.
  1. Sell the shares immediately: Exit the position. The trader takes the loss (or gain) relative to the current market price and frees up the capital.

The choice depends on the trader's view on the stock, the trader's portfolio allocation, and the trader's tax situation.

The Hold Decision

Holding the shares is appropriate when:

  • The trader has a long-term bullish view on the stock
  • The effective cost basis is significantly below the current market price
  • The trader has the portfolio allocation available for the stock
  • The stock pays a dividend (the dividend adds to the total return)

The hold decision converts the CSP from an income strategy to a stock purchase. The trader is now a stockholder, with all the risks and rewards of stock ownership.

The Covered Call Decision

Selling a covered call after the assignment is appropriate when:

  • The trader has a neutral to slightly bullish view on the stock
  • The IV is elevated (premiums are meaningful)
  • The trader wants to generate additional income on the shares
  • The trader is willing to cap the upside at the strike

The covered call strike should be above the effective cost basis. For a $93.50 cost basis, the call strike should be at least $95 (above the cost basis) to ensure a profit if assigned.

The Sell Decision

Selling the shares immediately after assignment is appropriate when:

  • The trader does not want to hold the stock (the original plan was income, not stock ownership)
  • The current market price is above the effective cost basis (the trader can sell at a gain)
  • The capital is needed for other opportunities
  • The trader has a bearish view on the stock

The sell decision closes the position. The trader's profit is the difference between the current market price and the effective cost basis, plus the original put premium.

The Wash Sale Rule

The wash sale rule applies if the trader sells the stock at a loss and repurchases the same stock within 30 days. The loss is disallowed for tax purposes, and the cost basis of the new shares is adjusted.

For a CSP that is assigned and the trader sells the stock at a loss within 30 days:

  • The loss on the stock sale is disallowed
  • The cost basis of the new shares is the sale price plus the disallowed loss
  • The holding period of the new shares includes the holding period of the original shares

The wash sale rule is a complication for traders who flip the stock quickly after assignment. The trader who plans to hold the stock long-term (or sell at a gain) does not have to worry about the wash sale rule.

The Cost Basis Tracking

The trader should track the cost basis carefully, especially for wheel strategies that involve multiple cycles. The tracking should include:

  • Each put premium (date, amount, holding period)
  • The strike at assignment
  • The stock purchase price (strike)
  • The cumulative premium
  • The effective cost basis
  • Any covered call premiums (if selling calls)
  • The cost basis after each call premium
  • The final sale price (if the stock is sold)

The tracking can be done in a spreadsheet, a tax software, or the broker's reporting tools. The trader who tracks carefully can verify the cost basis at any point and project the gain or loss on a potential sale.

The Common Mistakes

Selling the stock at a loss immediately after assignment. The trader sells the stock at the current market price, which is below the strike (otherwise the put would not have been assigned). The sale produces a loss. The trader should consider holding the stock and waiting for a recovery, or selling a covered call to generate income while waiting.

Failing to track the cost basis. The trader does not track the cumulative premium, and the cost basis is not updated after multiple cycles. The trader does not know the effective entry price and may make suboptimal decisions about whether to hold or sell.

Selling a covered call below the cost basis. The trader sells a covered call at a strike that is below the effective cost basis. The call is assigned, and the trader sells at a loss. The call strike should be above the cost basis.

Holding the stock through a major decline. The trader is assigned on a put, the stock continues to drop, and the trader holds the stock through a 30%+ decline. The cost basis is below the strike, but the stock has dropped below the cost basis. The trader should consider closing the position if the stock drops more than 15-20% from the cost basis.

Reinvesting the premium incorrectly. The trader receives the put premium as cash, but the cash is not reinvested in additional shares. The premium is "lost" to the trader. The wheel trader should reinvest the cash in additional CSPs or other income strategies.

The Post-Assignment Workflow

A practical workflow for handling CSP assignment:

  1. Verify the assignment in the broker's account. The 100 shares should be visible, and the cash reserve should be debited.
  2. Calculate the effective cost basis (strike - cumulative premium).
  3. Decide on the post-assignment action (hold, sell covered call, or sell).
  4. If holding: set a stop loss or a target sell price.
  5. If selling a covered call: choose a strike above the cost basis with 30-45 DTE.
  6. If selling: place a limit order at the target price.
  7. Track the position in the trader's records for tax and performance reporting.

The workflow is mechanical. The trader who follows it consistently handles the assignment in a way that maximizes the after-tax return and minimizes the risk of suboptimal decisions.

The CSP Assignment and the Wheel

The CSP assignment is a feature of the wheel strategy. The trader who runs the wheel expects to be assigned periodically, and the assignment is the transition from the put phase to the call phase.

The wheel workflow at assignment:

  1. Verify the assignment and calculate the cost basis.
  2. Sell a covered call at a strike above the cost basis (typically 3-5% above).
  3. Collect the call premium over the next 30-45 days.
  4. If the call expires worthless: sell a new call at the same strike.
  5. If the call is assigned: the stock is sold, and the trader returns to the put phase.

The wheel is a continuous loop. Each assignment is one phase of the loop, and the trader proceeds to the next phase.
