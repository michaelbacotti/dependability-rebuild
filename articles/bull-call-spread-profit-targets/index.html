Bull Call Spread Profit Targets: The 2x Debit Rule, Partial Profits, and Scaling Out

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The profit target for a bull call spread is one of the most discretionary decisions in the strategy. The trader can close at 50% of the debit, 100% of the debit, 200% of the debit, or at max profit. Each target has a different probability of being reached, a different expected value, and a different impact on the portfolio's return profile. The "right" target depends on the trader's view, the IV environment, and the time to expiration.

This article covers the standard profit target rules, the partial profit-taking strategy, the scaling-out approach, and the decision rule for selecting the target.

The Profit Target Decision

A bull call spread is entered with a debit. The position's value at any time is a function of the underlying's price and the time to expiration. The profit target is the value at which the position is closed.

Target Value at Close Probability of Reaching Expected Return per Trade
50% of debit $1.50 debit → $2.25 close 50-60% 12-15% of debit
100% of debit (2x) $1.50 debit → $3.00 close 30-40% 15-20% of debit
200% of debit (3x) $1.50 debit → $4.50 close 15-25% 15-25% of debit
Max profit $1.50 debit → $6.50 close 5-10% 5-10% of debit

The 50% target has the highest probability of being reached but the lowest expected return per trade. The max profit target has the lowest probability but a high payout. The 100% target is a balance between the two.

The 2x Debit Rule

The most common profit target for bull call spreads is the 2x debit rule: close the position when it reaches 2x the debit paid.

Example: Spread entered at $1.50 debit. The 2x target is reached when the spread is worth $3.00 (a 100% gain on the debit). At that point, the trader closes the position for a $1.50 profit per contract.

The 2x rule is popular because:

  • It is mechanical: the trader does not need to predict the underlying's move
  • It captures a meaningful gain: 100% of the debit is a strong return on a defined-risk trade
  • It gives the position room to work: the target is not at max profit, so the position can reach it even if the underlying does not reach the short strike

The 2x rule is appropriate for moderate-conviction trades where the trader wants a meaningful gain without holding for max profit.

The 50% Target

The 50% target closes the position when it reaches 50% of the debit paid. This is the most conservative target.

Example: Spread entered at $1.50 debit. The 50% target is reached when the spread is worth $2.25 (a 50% gain on the debit). At that point, the trader closes for a $0.75 profit per contract.

The 50% target is appropriate for:

  • Low-IV environments where the position's time value is small
  • High-correlation underlyings where the trader wants to lock in gains quickly
  • Multi-position portfolios where the trader wants to free up capital for other trades

The 50% target has a high probability of being reached (50-60%) but a low payout per trade. Over many trades, the 50% target produces a higher cumulative return than the 2x target because the win rate is higher.

The 3x Debit Target

The 3x target closes the position when it reaches 3x the debit paid. This is the most aggressive standard target.

Example: Spread entered at $1.50 debit. The 3x target is reached when the spread is worth $4.50 (a 200% gain on the debit).

The 3x target is appropriate for:

  • Event-driven trades (earnings, FDA, merger) where the underlying can move sharply in the expected direction
  • High-IV environments where the spread has significant upside
  • Trades with a long time to expiration (60+ DTE) where the position has time to reach the target

The 3x target has a low probability of being reached (15-25%) but a high payout per trade. Over many trades, the 3x target produces a higher return per winning trade but a lower win rate than the 50% or 2x targets.

The Max Profit Target

The max profit target closes the position when it reaches the spread's maximum value (the spread width minus the debit). This is the most patient target.

Example: $8-wide spread entered at $1.50 debit. Max profit is $6.50. The position is held until it reaches $6.50 (or until expiration).

The max profit target is appropriate for:

  • High-conviction trades where the trader has a strong view that the underlying will reach the short strike
  • Trades with significant time to expiration (60+ DTE)
  • Trades where the trader wants to capture the full value of the position

The max profit target has a very low probability of being reached (5-10%) and requires the underlying to move all the way to the short strike. Most traders who use this target end up closing at a smaller profit or a loss, because the underlying rarely reaches the short strike exactly.

Partial Profit-Taking

A common approach is to take partial profits at one target and close the remainder at a different target.

Example: 8-contract spread.

  • Close 4 contracts at 50% of debit ($2.25 per contract, $0.75 profit per contract = $300 profit)
  • Close the remaining 4 contracts at 2x debit ($3.00 per contract, $1.50 profit per contract = $600 profit)
  • Total profit if both targets reached: $900

If only the 50% target is reached, the trader locks in $300 in profit. If both targets are reached, the trader captures $900 in profit. The partial approach balances the high win rate of the 50% target with the higher payout of the 2x target.

Scaling Out

A more granular approach: close 1/3 of the position at each of three targets.

Example: 9-contract spread.

  • Close 3 contracts at 50% of debit
  • Close 3 contracts at 2x debit
  • Close 3 contracts at 3x debit

If all three targets are reached, the trader captures a blended return of (50% × 3) + (100% × 3) + (200% × 3) = $1,050 in profit per $1.50 debit × 9 contracts = $13.50 risk. That's an 7.7x return on the original risk — but only if all three targets are reached, which is rare.

The scaling-out approach is appropriate for traders who want to capture multiple profit levels without committing to a single target. The expected return is between the 50% and 3x targets, depending on the underlying's behavior.

The Time-Adjusted Target

The standard target rules assume a fixed profit level regardless of time. A more nuanced approach adjusts the target based on the time to expiration.

DTE Adjusted Target Rationale
45+ 3x debit Plenty of time; the position can reach a high target
30-45 2x debit Standard target; the position has time to work
21-30 50-100% of debit Time decay is accelerating; lock in gains
14-21 Close at any profit Final weeks; gamma risk is high
0-14 Hold to expiration No more time; let the position settle

The time-adjusted target is appropriate for traders who want to capture the most profit per trade without holding through the high-gamma final weeks. The adjustment can be applied to any of the standard targets (50%, 2x, 3x) by lowering the target as DTE decreases.

The Decision Rule

A practical algorithm for selecting the profit target:

  1. Check the IV regime:
    • Low IV: 50% target
    • Moderate IV: 2x target
    • Elevated IV: 2x-3x target
    • High IV (event): 3x target with defined exit before event
  2. Check the time to expiration:
    • 45+ DTE: 3x target acceptable
    • 30-45 DTE: 2x target standard
    • 21-30 DTE: 50-100% target
    • 0-21 DTE: close at any profit or hold to expiration
  3. Check the conviction level:
    • High conviction (clear catalyst): 3x target
    • Moderate conviction: 2x target
    • Low conviction: 50% target

The decision is environment-driven and time-driven. The trader who uses the 2x target as a default and adjusts based on IV and time will have a balanced return profile.

The Realized Return

A common mistake is to compare the profit target to the max profit (e.g., "the 2x target is only 25% of max profit, so I'm leaving money on the table"). The correct comparison is the profit target to the probability of reaching it.

  • 2x target: 100% of debit, reached 30-40% of the time → expected return per trade = 30-40% of debit
  • Max profit: 433% of debit ($6.50/$1.50), reached 5-10% of the time → expected return per trade = 22-43% of debit

The 2x target has a higher expected return per trade than the max profit target, despite the lower payout per winner. The win rate matters more than the per-trade payout.
