Iron Condor vs Iron Butterfly: Structure, Win Rate, Credit, and When to Use Each

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The iron condor and the iron butterfly are often discussed together because both are four-leg defined-risk premium-collection strategies that profit from time decay in range-bound markets. The structural difference between them — the width of the wings — produces meaningfully different risk profiles, credit characteristics, and management requirements. Choosing between them is not about which is "better"; it is about which is right for the IV environment, the size of the expected range, and the trader's tolerance for gamma risk.

Structural Comparison

An iron condor has a short spread and a long spread, with both spreads out of the money:

  • Short put spread: Sell a put at strike A, buy a put at strike A minus W (where W is the wing width)
  • Short call spread: Sell a call at strike B, buy a call at strike B plus W
  • Net credit: The premium received from selling both spreads minus the cost of buying the protective wings

An iron butterfly has a short straddle and a long straddle, with both straddles centered on the same strike:

  • Short straddle: Sell a put and a call at the same strike (typically at-the-money or just out-of-the-money)
  • Long straddle: Buy a put and a call at strikes equidistant from the short strike (the wings)
  • Net credit: The premium from the short straddle minus the cost of the long straddle

The iron butterfly is structurally a "centered" version of the iron condor — both have short strikes, but the butterfly's short strikes are closer to the current price of the underlying.

Credit and Win Rate

For the same underlying and the same DTE:

Structure Typical Credit Typical Win Rate Breakeven Width
Iron Condor (15-pt wings) $1.50 - $2.50 65-75% $12 - $15 (per side)
Iron Butterfly (5-pt wings) $3.00 - $5.00 50-60% $3 - $5 (per side)

The iron butterfly collects more credit in absolute terms but has a much narrower profit zone. The breakeven width of a butterfly is the wing width minus the credit, which is typically only a few points. A small move in the underlying can push the short strike into the money.

The iron condor collects less credit but has a much wider profit zone. A moderate move in the underlying (up to 1.0 standard deviation) is usually still within the profit zone. The condor's wider wings give the position more "room to be wrong."

Gamma Exposure

The iron butterfly is a high-gamma position. The short straddle is at or near the money, which means delta is highly sensitive to small moves. A butterfly trader is exposed to a sharp move in either direction. The condor trader has a much lower gamma exposure at entry because both short strikes are out of the money.

This gamma difference is the primary reason the butterfly has a lower win rate than the condor. The butterfly needs the underlying to stay in a tighter range to be profitable, and any meaningful move outside that range causes rapid losses.

Structure Gamma at Short Strike (45 DTE) Sensitivity to $5 Move
Iron Condor (15-pt wings) Low Small — delta changes a few cents per $5 move
Iron Butterfly (5-pt wings) High Large — delta can flip from neutral to short/long on a $5 move

When the Underlying Is Range-Bound

In a clear range-bound market, both strategies can be profitable, but the butterfly's higher credit and tighter breakeven can be appealing. The argument for the butterfly in a tight range is that the trader is collecting more premium for taking a more confident view on the range.

The argument against the butterfly is that the trader does not need to be that confident. The condor's wider wings give the position more tolerance for a slightly wider range, and the credit, while smaller, is more reliable. A condor that expires worthless collects 100% of its max profit; a butterfly that is tested but does not breach the long strike collects only a partial profit, and the partial profit may be less than the credit collected by a comparable condor.

When the Underlying Is Trending

In a trending market, neither strategy is ideal, but the condor is the better choice of the two. A condor with 15-point wings can absorb a moderate trend without breaching the short strike, and the wider profit zone gives the position time to manage. A butterfly with 5-point wings is at risk of being tested within days of entry, and the gamma exposure means that a small trend can produce a large mark-to-market loss.

Market Condition Preferred Structure Reason
Tight range, high confidence Iron Butterfly Higher credit, tighter breakeven rewards the conviction
Moderate range, lower confidence Iron Condor Wider profit zone, more tolerance for small trend days
Trending market Neither (close if held) Both strategies are directionally challenged; reduce size or exit
High IV, range-bound Iron Condor IV crush works on both; condor's wider wings handle the post-crush range expansion

Event-Driven Volatility

Both strategies can be used around events (FOMC, earnings) to collect the IV crush. The butterfly is more sensitive to the size of the move; a small surprise move can push the short strike into the money. The condor's wider wings give the position more tolerance for an unexpected move.

For event-driven trades, the condor is generally the better choice. The IV crush does the work that the structure would otherwise need, and the wider wings give the position the room to absorb a small surprise.

Capital Efficiency

The iron butterfly uses less buying power reduction (BPR) per contract than the iron condor in some brokers, but the trade-off is the higher gamma. A butterfly with a $5 wing has a max loss of $5 minus credit, while a condor with a $15 wing has a max loss of $15 minus credit. The condor's larger max loss is the price of the wider profit zone.

Structure Max Loss per Spread (SPX) Buying Power (per spread)
Iron Condor (15-pt wings) $12.50 - $13.50 $1,250 - $1,350
Iron Butterfly (5-pt wings) $2.00 - $3.00 $200 - $300

The condor's BPR is 4-5x the butterfly's, but the condor's win rate is also 10-15 percentage points higher.

Management Cost

The butterfly requires more active management than the condor. The narrow profit zone means that small moves produce meaningful mark-to-market changes, and the trader needs to be ready to roll or close quickly. The condor's wider wings give the position more "stay in the trade" room; a $5 move that would test a butterfly's short strike is still inside a condor's profit zone.

A useful heuristic: if the trader is not actively watching the position multiple times per day, the condor is the better structure. The butterfly requires hands-on management that not all traders can provide.

Choosing Between Them

The decision rule:

  • Default to the iron condor. It has a higher win rate, lower gamma, more management room, and a wider profit zone. The lower credit is the price of the lower risk.
  • Use the iron butterfly when the range is tight and conviction is high. A butterfly entered at 45 DTE on a stock that has traded in a $5 range for three weeks can collect meaningful premium with a high probability of profit. The narrow range makes the tight breakeven work.
  • Use the condor for event-driven trades. The wider wings give the position room to absorb an unexpected move, and IV crush does the work.
  • Use the condor for trending markets if a premium-collection trade is required. The condor's gamma profile is more forgiving of a moderate trend.

The butterfly is a specialty strategy. The condor is the workhorse. Most premium-collection programs should run 80%+ of their capital in condors and reserve the butterfly for high-conviction tight-range setups.
