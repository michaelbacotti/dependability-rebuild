Calendar Spread Near-Term Expiration Guide: What Happens and How to Manage the Long Leg

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

A calendar spread is held through a known event and typically closed 1-3 days after. But some calendar positions are held through the near-term option's expiration, either by design or because the trader missed the optimal exit window. At near-term expiration, the position's behavior changes significantly: the near-term option is essentially worthless (or about to expire), and the longer-dated option is the only meaningful leg. The decision at expiration is whether to close the position, roll the near-term option, or hold a long position in the longer-dated option.

What Happens at Near-Term Expiration

On the day of the near-term option's expiration, the position's profile has shifted dramatically:

Leg Pre-Expiration (T+5) At Expiration (T+0)
Short near-term option Has small time value Worthless (if OTM) or intrinsic (if ITM)
Long longer-dated option Has time value + some intrinsic Has time value + intrinsic
Net position Defined value Defined value minus near-term value

If the underlying is at or near the strike at expiration, the near-term option expires worthless and the trader keeps the full premium received at entry. The position is now essentially a long position in the longer-dated option.

If the underlying has moved beyond the strike, the near-term option has intrinsic value and the trader is assigned or buys the option back. The position's value is reduced by the intrinsic value of the near-term option.

The Three Outcomes at Expiration

The position can have three outcomes at near-term expiration:

  1. Stock at the strike (or near it): Near-term option expires worthless. Trader keeps the premium. Position is now a long longer-dated option.
  1. Stock above the strike (for call calendar): Near-term option has intrinsic value. Trader is assigned or buys back. Position's value is reduced by the intrinsic value. Trader is now long a longer-dated call with a lower effective cost basis.
  1. Stock below the strike (for call calendar): Near-term option expires worthless. Trader keeps the premium. Position is now a long longer-dated call. (Same as outcome 1 for a call calendar.)

The first outcome is the best case: the trader captures the full premium from the near-term option and has a long position in the longer-dated option for "free" (the cost was already paid at entry).

The Cost Basis After Expiration

After the near-term option expires, the trader's cost basis in the longer-dated option is the original debit paid for the calendar spread. The premium received from the near-term option is "realized" at expiration.

Example: Calendar spread with $3.00 debit. Near-term option expires worthless. Trader has a long position in the longer-dated option with a cost basis of $3.00.

If the longer-dated option is currently worth $5.00, the trader has an unrealized gain of $2.00 per spread. If the longer-dated option is worth $4.00, the trader has an unrealized gain of $1.00. The cost basis is preserved through the expiration.

The Decision at Expiration

At near-term expiration, the trader has three options:

  1. Close the position: Sell the longer-dated option at the current market price. The trader captures any gain or loss from the original calendar spread.
  1. Roll the near-term option: Close the current near-term option (which is essentially worthless) and open a new near-term option in the next expiration cycle. The calendar spread is now a "double calendar" with two near-term legs and one longer-dated leg.
  1. Hold the long leg: Do nothing. The trader now holds a long position in the longer-dated option, with the calendar spread's cost basis.

The choice depends on the trader's view on the underlying and the cost of the roll.

Closing the Position

Closing the position at expiration is the standard approach for traders who entered the calendar as a one-time IV crush trade. The trader sells the longer-dated option and captures the gain (if any).

Original Debit Longer-Dated Value at Expiration Profit/Loss
$3.00 $5.00 +$2.00
$3.00 $4.00 +$1.00
$3.00 $3.00 $0.00 (breakeven)
$3.00 $2.00 -$1.00

The longer-dated option's value at expiration depends on the time to expiration of the longer-dated option and the underlying's move. If the longer-dated option has 30 DTE remaining and the underlying is near the strike, the option is worth its time value (which is meaningful). If the longer-dated option has 7 DTE remaining and the underlying has moved, the option is worth its intrinsic value plus a small time value.

Rolling the Near-Term Option

Rolling the near-term option means closing the current near-term option (essentially worthless at expiration) and opening a new near-term option in the next expiration cycle. The result is a "double calendar" with two near-term legs and one longer-dated leg.

Example: Original calendar with 14 DTE short and 45 DTE long. At the 14 DTE expiration, the trader closes the 14 DTE option (worthless) and opens a new 14 DTE option in the next cycle. The position is now a 14 DTE short, a 30 DTE short, and a 45 DTE long.

The double calendar is more complex but allows the trader to maintain the position structure for a longer period. The cost of the roll is the new short option's premium.

Action Cost
Close current 14 DTE option $0 (worthless)
Open new 14 DTE option $2.00 (current 14 DTE premium)
Net cost of roll $2.00

The trader is effectively selling another 14 DTE option for $2.00 and adding that to the position. The longer-dated long leg is unchanged.

Holding the Long Leg

Holding the long leg is appropriate for traders who want to maintain a long volatility position in the underlying. The trader now has a long call (or put) with a cost basis equal to the original calendar spread's debit.

The held long leg behaves like a regular long option. The trader manages it as a directional or volatility position, with the calendar spread's cost basis providing a small "discount" on the entry.

For a calendar with a $3.00 debit on a $150 strike:

  • Original entry: $3.00 for the calendar
  • After near-term expiration: $3.00 cost basis in the longer-dated $150 call
  • Current value of longer-dated call: $5.00 (if 30 DTE remaining)
  • Unrealized gain: $2.00

The trader can close the long call for a $2.00 gain, or hold it as a long position with a cost basis of $3.00.

When to Roll vs Close vs Hold

The decision rule:

  1. If the longer-dated option is near max value and the trader wants to capture the gain: Close. Lock in the profit.
  1. If the trader wants to maintain the calendar structure for another event (e.g., next quarter's earnings): Roll. Open a new near-term option in the next cycle and continue the position.
  1. If the trader has a long-term view on the underlying and wants to maintain long exposure: Hold. Keep the longer-dated option as a long position.
  1. If the longer-dated option has little remaining value and the trader wants to exit: Close. The remaining value is small, and the cost of holding is the remaining time decay.

The Time Decay After Expiration

After the near-term option expires, the position is a single longer-dated option. The time decay of the longer-dated option depends on the time to expiration:

Longer-Dated DTE Daily Theta Interpretation
45+ -0.04 to -0.06 Moderate decay
30-45 -0.06 to -0.08 Peak decay
14-30 -0.05 to -0.07 High decay
7-14 -0.03 to -0.05 Lower decay
0-7 -0.01 to -0.03 Tail decay

The position is losing time value at a rate of 4-8 cents per day. The trader who holds the long leg for more than 30 days is giving back meaningful time value.

The Risk of Holding

Holding the long leg is not risk-free. The position is exposed to:

  • Time decay: The longer-dated option loses time value daily.
  • Vega risk: The position is long vega; an IV decline reduces the value.
  • Directional risk: The position has a delta that is meaningful if the underlying has moved from the strike.

The trader who holds the long leg should monitor these risks. A sharp move in the underlying or a sharp decline in IV can produce a meaningful loss.

The Calendar at Different Strikes

A calendar spread has both legs at the same strike. At expiration of the near-term option, the position is essentially a single longer-dated option at the original strike. The behavior of the long leg depends on whether the underlying is above or below the strike:

Underlying vs Strike Long Leg Value Interpretation
Below strike (for call) Time value only Long call with no intrinsic
At strike (for call) Time value Long ATM call
Above strike (for call) Intrinsic + time value Long ITM call with intrinsic

The trader who wants a directional position can choose the strike to match the view. A call calendar with a strike above the current price has a directional bias to the upside (the long leg has positive delta).

The Double Calendar

A double calendar is created by rolling the near-term option at expiration. The structure is:

  • Short near-term option (newly added)
  • Short near-term option (previously the longer-dated near-term, now closer to expiration)
  • Long longer-dated option (unchanged)

The double calendar collects premium from two short legs and pays time decay on two short legs. The net theta is higher (more positive) than a single calendar, but the cost is also higher.

The double calendar is appropriate for traders who want to maintain the IV crush exposure for multiple events. The cost is the additional near-term option's premium.

The Practical Decision at Expiration

For a trader who entered the calendar as a one-time IV crush trade, the standard approach is:

  1. Close the position 1-3 days after the event (T+2 to T+3), capturing the IV crush profit.
  2. If the position is held to near-term expiration (by accident or design), close at expiration unless the trader has a specific reason to roll or hold.

The "specific reason" to roll is the existence of another event in the near future. If the next quarter's earnings is 60-90 days away, the trader can roll the near-term option and maintain the position structure for the next event.

The Tax and Accounting Implications

At expiration, the near-term option is either:

  • Worthless (if OTM): the trader keeps the premium, no taxable event
  • Intrinsic (if ITM): the trader is assigned or buys back, generating a realized gain or loss

The longer-dated option remains in the account. Its cost basis is the original debit paid for the calendar spread.

The accounting depends on whether the broker treats the calendar as a single position or as two separate options. Most brokers treat the calendar as two separate options, with the debit recorded as the cost basis of the long leg (and the short leg's premium as a credit).
