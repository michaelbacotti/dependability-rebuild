Covered Call vs Cash-Secured Put Comparison: When to Use Each

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The covered call and the cash-secured put (CSP) are the two foundational income strategies in options trading. Both collect premium in exchange for an obligation; the difference is whether the trader is selling upside (covered call) or downside protection (cash-secured put). The two strategies have different capital requirements, different return profiles, and different use cases. Choosing between them is not about which is "better"; it is about which fits the trader's view on the stock and the trader's portfolio.

Structural Comparison

Covered call: Own 100 shares of the underlying stock and sell a call option at a strike above the current price. The premium is collected upfront. If the stock stays below the strike at expiration, the call expires worthless and the trader keeps the premium. If the stock rises above the strike, the trader is obligated to sell the shares at the strike.

Cash-secured put: Sell a put option at a strike below the current price and hold enough cash to buy 100 shares at the strike. The premium is collected upfront. If the stock stays above the strike at expiration, the put expires worthless and the trader keeps the premium. If the stock drops below the strike, the trader is obligated to buy the shares at the strike.

The covered call obligates the trader to sell at the strike. The CSP obligates the trader to buy at the strike. The two obligations are mirror images of each other.

Capital Requirements

Strategy Capital Required Liquidity
Covered call 100 shares × current price (e.g., $10,000 for a $100 stock) Low (shares are illiquid)
Cash-secured put Strike × 100 in cash (e.g., $9,500 for a $95 strike) High (cash is liquid)

The covered call requires the trader to own the stock, which is illiquid capital. The CSP requires the trader to hold cash, which is liquid but earns a low yield.

The capital efficiency:

  • For a $100 stock with a $95 put strike: covered call ties up $10,000 in shares, CSP ties up $9,500 in cash.
  • The covered call is more capital-intensive (because the current price is higher than the strike), but the capital is in the form of an appreciating asset.
  • The CSP is less capital-intensive, but the capital is in the form of cash that earns a low yield.

Return Comparison

Both strategies collect premium upfront, but the maximum return differs:

Strategy Maximum Return Realization
Covered call Premium + (Strike - Cost Basis) per share At call expiration if assigned
Cash-secured put Premium (if put expires worthless) At put expiration if OTM

For a $100 stock with a $105 call strike and a $95 put strike, both with 30 DTE and $1.50 premium:

  • Covered call max return: $1.50 (premium) + $5.00 (strike - cost basis) = $6.50 per share (6.5% on $100 cost basis in 30 days)
  • CSP max return: $1.50 (premium) per share (1.5% on $9,500 cash in 30 days)

The covered call has a higher max return (6.5% vs 1.5%) because the trader owns the stock and participates in the upside. The CSP has a lower max return because the trader's capital is in cash, not in the stock.

Risk Comparison

Risk Factor Covered Call Cash-Secured Put
Downside risk Full stock decline (offset by premium) Strike - stock price (offset by premium)
Upside risk Capped at strike None (trader doesn't own the stock)
Assignment risk Stock called away if above strike Stock assigned if below strike
Liquidity risk Stock is illiquid Cash is liquid
Ex-dividend risk Early assignment None (trader doesn't own stock)

The covered call has more risk than the CSP. The covered call trader is exposed to the full stock decline (minus the premium collected). The CSP trader is exposed only to the difference between the strike and the stock price (minus the premium collected).

For a $100 stock that drops to $80:

  • Covered call loss: $100 - $80 - $1.50 (premium) = $18.50 per share (18.5% loss)
  • CSP loss: $95 (strike) - $80 (current) - $1.50 (premium) = $13.50 per share (effectively on $9,500 cash = ~142% of the put premium, but capped at $95 - $1.50 = $93.50 per share if the stock goes to zero)

The CSP's max loss is the cash reserve minus the premium ($9,500 - $150 = $9,350 per contract). The covered call's max loss is the full stock value minus the premium, which is larger in absolute terms.

When to Use the Covered Call

The covered call is appropriate when:

  1. The trader already owns the stock (long-term holder looking to generate income).
  2. The trader has a neutral to slightly bullish view (the stock is expected to stay flat or rise modestly).
  3. The trader wants to reduce the cost basis of an existing position.
  4. The IV is elevated (premiums are meaningful, and the trader is willing to cap the upside).

The covered call is not appropriate when:

  • The trader expects a large upside move (the call caps the upside).
  • The trader is bearish on the stock (the covered call does not protect against downside).
  • The trader does not have the cash to buy 100 shares (the strategy requires share ownership).

When to Use the Cash-Secured Put

The CSP is appropriate when:

  1. The trader wants to acquire the stock at a discount (the effective cost basis is the strike minus the premium).
  2. The trader has a neutral to slightly bullish view (the stock is expected to stay above the strike).
  3. The trader has the cash available and is willing to deploy it for the strike purchase.
  4. The IV is elevated (premiums are meaningful, and the trader is willing to buy the stock at the strike).

The CSP is not appropriate when:

  • The trader is bearish on the stock (the CSP is a bullish-to-neutral strategy).
  • The trader does not have the cash to back the put (margin is not appropriate for a true CSP).
  • The trader wants upside exposure (the CSP does not participate in the stock's upside).

The Decision Rule

A practical algorithm for choosing between the strategies:

  1. Does the trader already own the stock?
    • Yes: covered call. Generate income on the existing position.
    • No: consider CSP (or buy the stock first).
  2. Does the trader want to acquire the stock?
    • Yes: CSP. Get paid to wait for the entry.
    • No: covered call (if already owned) or skip the strategy.
  3. What is the trader's view on the stock?
    • Bullish: CSP (better entry if assigned) or buy the stock outright.
    • Neutral: covered call (if owned) or CSP (to acquire at a discount).
    • Bearish: do not use either strategy.
  4. What is the IV environment?
    • High IV: both strategies collect more premium.
    • Low IV: both strategies collect less premium; consider skipping.

The decision is view-driven. The trader who has a bullish view and wants to acquire the stock uses the CSP. The trader who already owns the stock and has a neutral view uses the covered call.

The Equivalent Positions

A covered call at strike X on a stock at price P is economically equivalent to a CSP at strike X on a stock at price P, IF the covered call trader bought the stock at price P. The two positions have the same profit/loss profile.

Outcome Covered Call at $105 Strike CSP at $95 Strike (Stock at $100)
Stock at $100 (no change) +$1.50 premium +$1.50 premium
Stock at $110 (up 10%) $5.00 gain + $1.50 premium = $6.50 $1.50 premium (put expires worthless)
Stock at $90 (down 10%) -$10.00 loss + $1.50 premium = -$8.50 -$5.00 loss (assigned at $95) + $1.50 premium = -$3.50

The covered call and the CSP are not exactly equivalent in this case (because the strikes are different), but the structure is similar. A covered call at $105 and a CSP at $95 are both "income strategies" that collect premium in exchange for an obligation.

The Synthetic Equivalence

A covered call can be replicated synthetically with a CSP and a long stock position. The two strategies are equivalent in their payoff diagrams, but they have different capital requirements and different margin treatments.

For a trader with $10,000:

  • Covered call: buy 100 shares at $100, sell a $105 call for $1.50.
  • Synthetic covered call: sell a $95 CSP for $1.50 (backs the obligation), and the $10,000 cash is the equivalent of the stock position.

The synthetic covered call frees up the cash that would have been used to buy the stock. The trade-off is the obligation to buy the stock at $95 if the put is assigned.

The choice between the real and synthetic versions depends on the trader's account type and preferences.

The Account Type Considerations

Account Type Covered Call CSP
Cash account Allowed (if shares are owned) Allowed (if cash is available)
Margin account Allowed (shares can be held on margin) Allowed (naked put on margin)
IRA Allowed (if shares are owned) Allowed (naked put in some IRAs)
Roth IRA Allowed Some restrictions (depends on broker)

The CSP is the primary strategy for IRA accounts that do not allow naked puts. The covered call is the primary strategy for taxable accounts where the trader already owns the stock.

The Portfolio Allocation

A typical income portfolio might allocate:

Component Allocation Strategy Purpose
Stock holdings 50-70% Covered calls Income on existing positions
Cash 20-40% Cash-secured puts Income on cash, potential stock acquisition
Other 10-20% Other strategies Diversification

The 50-70% in covered calls assumes the trader has a substantial stock portfolio. The 20-40% in CSPs assumes the trader has cash available for new positions. The 10-20% in other strategies provides diversification.

The Common Mistakes

Using a covered call on a stock in a sustained uptrend. The trader sells a covered call on a stock that is about to make a large move. The call caps the upside, and the trader misses the move. The covered call is appropriate for neutral-to-slightly-bullish stocks, not for strong uptrends.

Using a CSP on a stock in a sustained downtrend. The trader sells a put on a stock that is dropping. The put is assigned, and the trader buys the stock at a price above the current market. The CSP is appropriate for neutral-to-slightly-bullish stocks, not for downtrends.

Selling calls with strikes below the cost basis. The trader sells a covered call at a strike that is below the cost basis. The call is assigned, and the trader sells at a loss. The strike should be above the cost basis to ensure a profit if assigned.

Selling puts with strikes that are not attractive as entry points. The trader sells a CSP at a strike that is above what they would want to pay for the stock. The put is assigned, and the trader overpays. The strike should be a price the trader would be comfortable paying for the stock.
