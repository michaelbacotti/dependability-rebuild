Iron Condor DTE Timing: 30 vs 45 vs 60 Days to Expiration

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

The choice of days to expiration at entry is one of the most consequential decisions in iron condor construction. Enter too early and you collect less premium per day of exposure. Enter too late and the position has too little time to recover from a directional drift. The 30/45/60 DTE question is not about which is "best" — it is about which is right for a given IV environment, capital allocation, and management capacity.

This article compares 30, 45, and 60 DTE entries on premium, theta profile, gamma exposure, win rate, and management cost, and lays out the decision rule for selecting the right DTE band.

The Three Standard Entry Points

Most retail iron condors are entered with one of three expirations:

  • 30 DTE: The position has just over a month to expiration. Theta is high and accelerating. Gamma is low. The position can be entered with tighter strikes because there is less time for a large move.
  • 45 DTE: The standard institutional entry. Theta is high but not yet peaking. Gamma is low. The position has more room to manage a directional drift, and the credit-to-width ratio is typically in the 20-30% range.
  • 60 DTE: A longer-duration entry. Theta is starting to accelerate from a lower base. Gamma is very low. Premium collected is higher in absolute terms but lower per day of exposure.

The 45 DTE entry is the most common for a reason: it balances premium collection against gamma risk and gives the position enough time to work through a small directional drift.

Premium Comparison

For a 15-point wide SPX condor with short strikes roughly 1.0 standard deviation out of the money:

| DTE | Typical Credit | Credit per Day Held |

|---|---|---|

| 30 | $1.00 - $1.50 | $0.033 - $0.050 |

| 45 | $1.50 - $2.20 | $0.033 - $0.049 |

| 60 | $1.80 - $2.80 | $0.030 - $0.047 |

The credit per day held is roughly similar across the three entry points. The longer-dated entries collect more absolute premium but spread it over more days, so the daily rate is comparable. The choice between 30, 45, and 60 DTE is therefore not primarily a credit optimization question — it is a risk and management question.

Theta Profile

Theta is not constant across expirations. For an at-the-money option, theta is highest at 30-45 DTE, then declines as expiration approaches. For an out-of-the-money option (the short strikes of a condor), the theta profile is shifted — peak theta is closer to 45-60 DTE, with the back-loaded decay pattern that condor sellers rely on.

| DTE Band | Theta Pattern |

|---|---|

| 60 → 30 DTE | Theta accelerating. Position is collecting time decay at an increasing rate. |

| 30 → 14 DTE | Theta near peak. Position is collecting maximum daily premium. |

| 14 → 0 DTE | Theta declining. Premium remaining is small; gamma is now the dominant risk. |

The 45 DTE entry gives the position the most time in the "theta accelerating" phase (45 → 30 DTE is 15 days of acceleration) and the most time in the "theta at peak" phase (30 → 14 DTE is 16 days). A 30 DTE entry skips the acceleration phase and goes straight to peak. A 60 DTE entry extends the acceleration phase but at a lower daily rate.

Gamma Exposure

Gamma is the rate of change of delta. A high-gamma position is sensitive to small moves in the underlying; a low-gamma position is less sensitive. For an iron condor, gamma risk is concentrated in the short strikes, and it grows rapidly as expiration approaches.

| DTE | Gamma at Short Strike (Approximate) |

|---|---|

| 60 | Low — small changes in underlying produce small changes in delta |

| 45 | Low — gamma is starting to rise but still manageable |

| 30 | Moderate — delta is starting to move with $5-10 changes in underlying |

| 14 | High — small moves can flip the short strike from out-of-the-money to in-the-money |

| 7 | Very high — gamma dominates; small moves can cause large mark-to-market swings |

The 30 DTE entry has materially more gamma risk than the 45 DTE entry. A 60 DTE entry has the least gamma risk at entry, but the position will still pass through 30 DTE on its way to expiration and will experience the same gamma exposure. The 45 DTE entry is the most efficient balance: enough time to manage, low enough gamma to avoid noise.

Win Rate and Management

Historical studies of SPX condors show win rates in the 65-75% range when the position is held to expiration or closed at 50% of max profit. The win rate is fairly stable across 30, 45, and 60 DTE entries when adjusted for the difference in strike width.

What changes is the management overhead:

  • 30 DTE entry: Less time to manage. Decisions come faster. If the position is threatened at 14 DTE, the only realistic adjustment is a 7-14 day roll forward at a debit. The window for rolling is narrow.
  • 45 DTE entry: The standard management timeline. At 21 DTE, decisions are made on whether to roll the untested side forward. At 14 DTE, the position is either profitable and on track to expire, or it needs to be closed.
  • 60 DTE entry: More time to manage. The position can absorb a multi-week directional drift that a 30 DTE entry could not. But the longer duration ties up capital and margin for longer.

The 45 DTE entry has the cleanest management timeline: 21 days to evaluate, 14 days to act, 7 days to close if needed. The 30 DTE entry compresses this timeline by a third, and the 60 DTE entry extends it but at the cost of more capital tied up.

IV Environment Considerations

The optimal DTE shifts with IV. In low-IV environments (VIX below 15, IV rank below 30), 60 DTE entries collect more premium and have a wider profit zone. In high-IV environments (VIX above 25, IV rank above 60), 30 DTE entries are often preferred because IV crush does the work that longer duration would otherwise do.

| IV Environment | Preferred Entry | Reason |

|---|---|---|

| VIX < 15, IV rank < 30 | 60 DTE | Need longer duration to collect meaningful premium; low gamma is the upside |

| VIX 15-25, IV rank 30-60 | 45 DTE | Standard environment; 45 DTE is the optimal balance |

| VIX > 25, IV rank > 60 | 30 DTE | IV crush accelerates premium collection; longer duration ties up capital that could be deployed elsewhere |

The "right" DTE is environment-dependent, and rigid rules ("always 45 DTE") underperform environment-adjusted rules.

The 45 DTE Default

For most retail and institutional iron condor programs, 45 DTE is the right default entry. It balances premium, gamma, and management overhead, and it gives the position enough time to work through a moderate directional drift without requiring constant attention. The exceptions are:

  • Low IV environments where longer duration is needed to make the credit meaningful
  • High IV environments where IV crush does the heavy lifting
  • Account sizes where 30 DTE entries allow more positions with the same margin

The 30 DTE entry is the second most common and is appropriate for high-conviction range-bound setups or for accounts that want to run more positions. The 60 DTE entry is the least common and is best reserved for low-IV environments or for traders who explicitly prefer longer-duration management timelines.
