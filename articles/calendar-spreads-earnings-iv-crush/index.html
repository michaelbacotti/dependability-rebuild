Calendar Spreads: Profiting from IV Crush Around Known Events

May 22, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

Before known events — earnings announcements, FDA decisions, mergers, earnings — implied volatility typically spikes as markets price in uncertainty. After the event, regardless of which direction the stock moves, IV collapses. This is the IV crush, and the calendar spread is one of the most elegant ways to profit from it with defined risk.

What Is a Calendar Spread?

A calendar spread (also called a time spread) involves:

  • Sell a near-term option at a specific strike
  • Buy the same strike and quantity in a later expiration
  • Same underlying, same strike — the only difference is the expiration

Net debit — you pay upfront. The profit comes from the near-term option losing value faster than the longer-dated option, capturing the IV differential.

Example:

  • Stock at $150, earnings in 2 weeks
  • Sell $150 call expiring in 2 weeks for $3.00 (near-term IV elevated)
  • Buy $150 call expiring in 8 weeks for $5.00 (longer-dated retains more value)
  • Net debit: $2.00 (=$200 per spread)

Post-earnings, stock moves to $160:

  • Near-term $150 call: worth ~$10 at expiration (intrinsic value), but IV collapsed so little extra → bought back for ~$10 → net $0 on this leg (you sold it at $3, bought it back at $10, loss of $7)
  • Longer-dated $150 call: worth ~$12 (still has time, IV partially retained) → sell it for $12 → net +$7 on this leg (bought at $5, sold at $12)
  • Net: -$7 (near-term) + +$7 (longer-dated) ≈ breakeven on the directional move

The calendar profits when IV crushes without a large directional move. The near-term option collapses fast and cheap to close; the longer-dated retains enough value to more than offset it.

Better scenario — stock stays flat at $150:

  • Near-term $150 call: worth ~$0 (expires worthless, no intrinsic, IV crushed) → bought back for $0 → net +$3 on this leg (sold at $3, bought back for $0)
  • Longer-dated $150 call: worth ~$5 (no intrinsic, some IV retained, still has time) → sell it for $5 → net $0 on this leg (bought at $5, sold at $5)
  • Net: +$3 (near-term closed) + $0 (longer-dated) = $3 profit on $2 debit = 150% return

Why Calendar Over Buying Naked Options?

Buying naked options before earnings is high-risk: you need the stock to move far enough to overcome the elevated IV, and if the move doesn't happen, you lose the full premium.

Calendar spreads solve this by:

  1. Selling the elevated IV — you're on the other side of the IV spike
  2. Defined risk — max loss is the net debit paid
  3. Directionally neutral — you profit whether the stock goes up or down, as long as the IV crush is large enough

The math:

  • Stock at $150, earnings in 2 weeks, near-term IV elevated (60th+ percentile)
  • Selling a naked $150 call: premium received = $3.00 (you collect this upfront)
  • The naked option's profit depends on whether the stock moves far enough to offset the elevated premium
  • With a calendar: sell 2-week $150 call for $3.00, buy 8-week $150 call for $5.00
  • Net debit: $2.00. Profit comes from the near-term collapsing faster than the longer-dated
  • The near-term option loses time value faster; the longer-dated retains proportionally more of its IV
  • This is why the calendar is the preferred strategy around events — you profit from IV collapse with defined risk, not directional betting.

When to Use It

Ideal conditions:

  • Known event with high probability (earnings, FDA decision, merger deadline)
  • IV rank above 60th percentile going into the event
  • Uncertain direction — you know something will move but not which way
  • You want defined risk with a known event catalyst

The IV crush logic:

Before the event: IV is high (expensive options)

After the event: IV normalizes (cheap options)

Calendar spread profits because you sold the expensive near-term and bought the cheaper longer-dated. When IV crushes, the near-term collapses more in absolute terms than the longer-dated, and you keep the difference.

Avoid when:

  • IV rank is below 30th percentile — not enough premium to sell
  • No clear event catalyst — calendar needs the IV spike to be profitable
  • Near-term IV is extremely elevated (>90th percentile) — may not collapse enough

Entry Logic

Strike selection:

  1. ATM is most common — at-the-money strikes have the most vega, so they benefit most from IV differential
  2. Slightly OTM works — if you have a directional lean, OTM gives you some upside participation
  3. Avoid deep ITM — intrinsic value dominates, time value (and IV sensitivity) is lower

Sizing the position:

  • Example: $100K portfolio, 2% max risk = $2,000 per calendar spread
  • Net debit of $2.00 per spread → 10 spreads max, run 3-5
  • Spread across different strikes to reduce concentration risk

Entry timing:

  • Enter 4-6 weeks before the event (to buy the longer-dated far enough out)
  • Or enter 2-3 weeks before the event (near-term IV already elevated)
  • Earlier entry captures more of the IV buildup but costs more for the longer-dated leg

Exit Rules

Taking profit:

  • Close the day after the event once IV crush is complete (typically 1-3 days post-event)
  • The near-term option will be worth very little if IV has normalized
  • The longer-dated option retains value — sell it to close

Specific rules:

  • If IV crush is complete (near-term IV normalized to pre-event levels): close both legs
  • If the stock moves significantly against you (>10% move): close early, thesis may be wrong
  • If 50% loss of net debit before event: evaluate and close or roll

Rolling:

  • If the position is losing and you still like the setup: buy back the near-term, sell a new near-term further out
  • This converts the spread from one near-term to the next
  • Only do this if you still have high confidence in the event catalyst

Greeks Characteristics

At entry with 3 weeks to near-term expiration and 8 weeks to far-term:

  • Delta: Near zero — long and short at same strike, deltas offset
  • Theta: Mixed — near-term loses time fast (positive for your short), long-term loses slower (negative for your long). Net theta is slightly negative early; becomes positive as near-term accelerates toward expiration
  • Vega: Positive — the long far-term has more vega than the short near-term; net is long volatility
  • Gamma: Long gamma near expiration of near-term — the spread benefits from large moves at expiration

The Greeks shift as near-term expiration approaches:

  • 3 weeks out: slightly negative theta, positive vega
  • 1 week out: theta turns positive, gamma rises
  • At near-term expiration: maximum theta gain as near-term expires worthless

Common Mistakes

1. Wrong strike selection

ATM is the most reliable because it captures the maximum IV differential. OTM calendars are speculative — you're betting on a specific move direction.

Fix: Stick to ATM unless you have a strong directional thesis. If you do, an OTM calendar is a compromise between a spread and a naked call.

2. Holding the long side through a second event

If you hold the longer-dated option through another major event, IV can crush the long side unexpectedly.

Fix: Close the calendar spread before any second major event on the same underlying.

3. Not closing after IV crush

After earnings, IV normalizes within days. If you hold the spread for weeks hoping for more, you're fighting theta decay on the long side.

Fix: Set a rule: close 1-3 days after the event, no matter what the stock does.

4. Position sizing too large

The calendar spread's profit is the IV differential, not the directional move. If IV doesn't crush enough, you can lose the full debit.

Fix: 2% max of capital per calendar. Spread across 3-5 different strikes to reduce single-strike risk.

5. Entering when front-load IV is too low

Below 40th percentile IV rank means the premium isn't high enough to make the calendar profitable.

Fix: Wait for IV rank above 50-60th percentile before entering.
