Bull Call Spreads: Complete Strategy Guide for Moderate Bullish Moves

May 20, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

When you expect a moderate move higher — enough to be profitable but not a runaway move — a bull call spread offers defined-risk directional exposure at a fraction of the cost of buying naked calls. Buying a naked call is expensive in elevated IV environments; the spread reduces cost while maintaining upside participation.

What Is a Bull Call Spread?

A bull call spread consists of:

Advertisement
  • Buy a call at a lower strike (closer to current price)
  • Sell a call at a higher strike (above your target)
  • Same expiration for both legs
  • Net debit — you pay upfront; the short call partially funds the long call

The max profit is the difference between the strikes minus the net debit paid. The max loss is simply the net debit paid.

Example:

  • Stock at $100
  • Buy $105 call for $3.00
  • Sell $115 call for $1.00
  • Net debit: $2.00 (=$200 per spread)
  • Max profit: $10 (strike width) - $2.00 (debit) = $8.00 (=$800 per spread)
  • Max loss: $2.00 (=$200 per spread)
  • Breakeven: $107 (buy strike + net debit)

Why Use a Spread Instead of a Naked Call?

Buying a naked call in elevated IV environments is expensive. When implied volatility is high, out-of-the-money calls are priced with a significant premium that works against you if the stock doesn't move aggressively.

The IV problem:

  • Stock at $100, IV 80th percentile
  • 30-day $110 call might cost $4.00
  • Stock moves 5% to $105 — the call might only gain $1.50 in value
  • You lost money despite being right on direction

The spread solution:

  • Same stock at $100, IV 80th percentile
  • Buy $105 call for $2.50
  • Sell $115 call for $0.75
  • Net debit: $1.75 (=$175 per spread)
  • Stock moves 5% to $105 → $105 call worth ~$5 (intrinsic value)
  • $115 call expires worthless (stock below $115)
  • Net spread value: $5 (long) - $0 (short expired) = $5
  • Profit: $5.00 - $1.75 = $3.25 on $1.75 risk = 186% return
  • The short call funded the long call, reducing the cost of directional exposure while keeping the upside.

Key Metrics

Parameter Target Rationale
Strike selection 2–3 strikes below expected move Captures moderate move without needing explosive rally
Days to expiration (DTE) 30–45 days Theta decay accelerates here; sufficient time for move to develop
Net debit No more than 50% of intrinsic value of long call Ensures reasonable risk/reward; avoid paying full intrinsic value
Position size No more than 2% of capital per spread Manage tail risk; spreads can still lose 100% of debit
Max profit target 2x debit paid Realistic take-profit level for moderate moves

When to Use It

Ideal conditions:

  • Moderate bullish outlook (5–15% expected move)
  • IV elevated or rising (but not extremely high)
  • You have a specific price target in mind
  • Want defined risk with limited capital at risk

Better alternatives in other scenarios:

  • Strong explosive move expected: Buy naked call or debit call spread with wider width
  • Very low IV: Long call alone is cheaper; the spread doesn't add enough value
  • Neutral/sideways: Bull call spread is wrong tool — use iron condor or bull put spread
  • Bearish: Bear call spread or bear put spread

Entry Logic

Strike selection process:

  1. Identify your price target — where do you expect the stock to be at expiration?
  2. Subtract the net debit — this is your breakeven, not your target
  3. Place the short strike at or slightly above your target — this maximizes credit and keeps debit reasonable
  4. Place the long strike below your target — gives the stock room to move into the profit zone

Example with a real target:

  • Stock at $150, expect move to $165 in 6 weeks
  • Breakeven at $155 ($150 + $5 debit)
  • Buy $155 call, sell $175 call
  • Debit = $5.00, max profit = $15 ($20 width - $5 debit)
  • This requires a 10% move to hit the profit zone — reasonable for a 6-week horizon

Sizing the position:

  • Determine max loss (net debit per spread)
  • Risk no more than 1-2% of portfolio per position
  • Example: $100K portfolio, 2% max = $2,000 per position
  • If each spread costs $200 debit, max 10 spreads... but run 3-5 at a time across correlated names

Entry timing:

  • Enter when you have high confidence in the catalyst (earnings, FDA date, product launch)
  • Enter when IV is high enough to make the spread cheap relative to naked call
  • Avoid entering when the stock has already gapped up 5%+ in a day — the move may be exhausted

Position Management

Taking profit:

  • Standard exit: close when profit = 2x the debit paid
  • Example: debit $2.00 → take profit at $4.00 (100% return)
  • This captures most of the move without holding to expiration

Rolling the short strike:

  • If the stock breaks through your short strike and you still like the position
  • Buy back the short call, sell a new higher-strike call (same expiration)
  • Collect additional credit, extend the position's runway
  • Do this only if the fundamental thesis hasn't changed

Managing losers:

  • If the stock moves against you (drops), the spread loses value
  • Do not hold past 2 weeks before expiration — time decay accelerates
  • Evaluate: is the thesis broken (close) or just early (hold)?
  • A drop to the long strike with 3+ weeks left is not a reason to close

Stop-out rules:

  • If stock drops below the long strike with >2 weeks remaining and thesis is intact: hold
  • If stock drops and you lose confidence in the thesis: close at 2x max loss
  • If 1 week from expiration and still significantly underwater: close or roll

Greeks Characteristics

At 30-45 DTE with moderate upside bias:

  • Delta: Positive — long call dominates, net delta is positive (0.30–0.50 for a 10-wide spread)
  • Theta: Slightly negative early — time decay costs on the long leg but is partially offset by short call decay
  • Vega: Slightly positive — long call has more vega than short call, so net is long volatility
  • Gamma: Positive — the spread benefits from large moves in either direction near expiration

Key insight: The bull call spread is slightly long volatility. If IV rises while the stock moves, you get a double benefit.

Common Mistakes

1. Buying spreads that are too wide

A 20-wide spread looks more profitable but costs more and requires a bigger move.

Fix: Use tighter spreads (5-10 wide) to reduce debit and improve breakeven.

2. Holding through expiration

Time decay accelerates in the last 2 weeks. If the spread is underwater, holding to expiration guarantees the loss.

Fix: Close at 2 weeks DTE if significantly underwater and thesis hasn't materialized.

3. Not taking profit at 2x

Holding hoping for max profit often leads to giving back gains.

Fix: Take 50% profit when you can. If you expect more, close half and let the rest run.

4. Entry when IV is low

The spread's advantage is the short call funding the long call. If IV is low, the short call doesn't provide enough credit.

Fix: Wait for IV to rise above 40th percentile before entering bullish spreads.

5. Oversizing on a single trade

Bull call spreads can lose 100% of the debit. If you allocate 20% of capital to one spread and it goes wrong, you devastate your account.

Fix: 1-2% of capital per position. Run 3-5 positions across different tickers for diversification.
