Iron Condor Rolling: When to Roll, When to Close, and How to Manage the Adjustment

June 04, 2026  |  Dependability Research Desk

An iron condor that is threatened by a directional move does not have to be closed at a loss. Rolling one or both spreads forward in time — and, depending on the situation, to different strikes — is the standard adjustment technique. The question is not whether rolling is possible; it is when rolling is the right move, when closing is the better move, and how to structure the roll so that the math works in your favor rather than against you.

Why Roll an Iron Condor

The thesis for an iron condor is that the underlying will stay in a range until expiration. When the underlying drifts toward one of the short strikes, the position moves from a winner toward a loser. There are three responses:

  1. Hold and hope — accept the directional risk in exchange for collecting the remaining premium. This works when the move is shallow and the underlying can revert, and it fails badly when the move is structural.
  2. Close the position — accept the loss and exit. This is the right call when the original thesis is broken, when the move is large enough that reversion is unlikely, or when capital is needed elsewhere.
  3. Roll the threatened side — move the spread forward in time, and often to different strikes, to give the position more time and a wider profit zone.

Rolling is the third path, and it is the most misunderstood. A roll is not free. It costs either additional capital (a debit roll) or it sacrifices potential profit (a credit roll with a worse breakeven). The roll's job is to convert an uncertain near-term loss into a more manageable position with a higher probability of eventual profit, not to erase the loss.

The 21 DTE Rule of Thumb

Most iron condors are entered with 30 to 45 days to expiration. The most common adjustment window is around 21 days to expiration, for two reasons:

  • Theta acceleration has tapered. At 30 DTE, theta is high and working hard for the seller. By 21 DTE, theta has done most of the work it is going to do for the position, and gamma is starting to take over on the threatened side.
  • Decisions become binary. A condor at 21 DTE with the underlying between the short strikes is almost certainly going to expire worthless and is best left alone. A condor at 21 DTE with the underlying at or beyond a short strike is in trouble, and the choice is to roll or to close.

A useful rule: at 21 DTE, the position either wins or it needs adjustment. There is not much middle ground.

When to Roll: The Decision Framework

A roll is appropriate when three conditions are met:

  1. The original thesis still has merit. The underlying has drifted, but the range is still expected to hold through the new expiration date. If the move is the start of a new trend, rolling only delays the loss.
  2. The threatened side is at or just beyond the short strike. If the underlying is still inside the short strike, no adjustment is needed. If the underlying has blown through the long strike (the protection), rolling is rarely the right answer — closing is.
  3. The cost of rolling is reasonable. A roll that costs more than 1.0x the original credit is rarely worth it. A roll that costs less than 0.5x the original credit is usually a good trade.

If any of these three conditions is not met, close the position.

Debit Rolls vs Credit Rolls

A roll can be structured in two ways:

Debit roll: Close the current spread for a debit, then open a new spread at a different strike and/or expiration for a larger or equal debit. The net effect is that you pay additional capital to extend the position. Debit rolls are common when rolling the untested side forward to collect more premium, or when rolling the tested side to a wider strike to give the position more room.

Credit roll: Close the current spread for a credit and open a new spread for a smaller credit. The net effect is that you collect a small amount of additional premium in exchange for accepting a worse breakeven on the new position. Credit rolls are common when rolling the tested side forward without changing strikes — the position collects a few more days of decay in exchange for accepting that the underlying is now closer to the strike.

Practical guidance: Most adjustments should be credit rolls when possible, because they add premium to the position. Debit rolls should be reserved for situations where a credit roll would leave the position structurally weak (for example, a credit roll that puts the new short strike inside the tested range).

Rolling the Untested Side

If the underlying has drifted toward the call side (toward your short call), the put spread is still well out of the money and is collecting theta. The untested-side adjustment rolls the put spread forward to a later expiration, collecting additional premium and giving the call side more time to manage.

Example: SPX at 5,820, original 30-DTE condor with short call at 5,900 and short put at 5,700. Underlying drifts to 5,890 — short call is now in the money zone. The put spread is still 120 points away from being tested.

Roll the put spread from 30 DTE to 60 DTE. The 60 DTE put spread will collect additional credit because of the longer time to expiration. The net effect is more premium in the position, more time for the call side to recover, and unchanged risk on the put side.

This is the cleanest adjustment and should be the first one to consider when one side is threatened but the other side is comfortably out of the money.

Rolling the Tested Side

If the underlying has moved to or through the short call, the call spread is the threatened side. Rolling the call spread forward in time, with or without changing strikes, is the standard adjustment.

Strike change: Roll the call spread to a higher short strike. For example, roll from a 5,900/5,950 call spread to a 5,950/6,000 call spread. This gives the position more room above the current price but costs additional debit because the new spread is wider.

No strike change: Roll the call spread to a later expiration at the same strikes. The new spread will collect a credit equal to the additional theta over the new time period. This is the cheapest adjustment but leaves the short strike closer to the current price.

A common compromise is to roll up by one strike and out by 30-45 days. The cost is a small debit, the new short strike is comfortably above the current price, and the position has more time to work.

The Common Mistakes

Rolling too early. Adjusting at 30 DTE when the underlying is just barely near a strike adds cost without changing the outcome. The position needs room to work, and rolling when there is no immediate threat reduces premium collected.

Rolling the wrong side. When the underlying is near the short call, the instinct is sometimes to roll the call side forward to collect more time decay on the threatened side. This makes the problem worse — the call side needs more time, not less. The correct adjustment is usually to roll the untested (put) side forward to give the call side more time.

Accepting unlimited debits. A roll that costs more than the original credit is rarely justified. The position has already given back a portion of the premium collected, and adding more capital on top of that loss is a sign that the original thesis is broken.

Rolling to a worse breakeven without realizing it. A credit roll to the same strikes and a later expiration gives up some breakeven room in exchange for premium. The new breakeven may be tighter than the current underlying price, which means the position cannot recover even with more time.

When to Close Instead

Close the position when:

  • The underlying has moved through the long strike (the protection), making the spread fully tested
  • The cost of rolling exceeds 1.0x the original credit
  • The underlying has gapped on news and the original range thesis is broken
  • A major event (FOMC, CPI) is within 5 trading days and the position is threatened
  • Capital is needed for a higher-conviction setup elsewhere

Closing is not a failure. It is the correct response to a thesis that no longer holds. Many traders lose money by holding condors too long, hoping for reversion, when the right move was to close at the first sign that the range had broken.
