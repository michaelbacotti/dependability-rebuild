Research Methodology & Disclosures

How we build market forecasts, where the data comes from, and the standards every published piece must clear.

Three-Pillar Forecast Framework

Every directional call on Dependability starts with three explicit inputs. We do not publish single-number targets without disclosing the underlying weighting, because single numbers hide the uncertainty that retail readers need in order to size positions responsibly.

Pillar 1 — Wall Street consensus. Major institutional price targets (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, Bank of America, Citi, Wells Fargo, Barclays, Deutsche Bank) aggregated into a consensus year-end S&P 500 target. Updated monthly from each firm's published outlook. Consensus gets the most weight because it reflects dedicated equity strategy teams with full access to company-level earnings models, sector rotation frameworks, and macro inputs.

Pillar 2 — Historical seasonality & pattern recognition. Decennial cycles, presidential-cycle patterns, sell-in-May statistics, Q4 volatility tendencies, and sector-rotation regimes drawn from 1990 onward. We use this pillar as a sanity check rather than a primary signal — markets evolve, and pure seasonal patterns can mislead when macro conditions differ from the historical base case. We flag which seasonal pattern we are citing and over what window it has historically applied.

Pillar 3 — Options market signals. VIX spot level, VIX term structure (VIX3M minus VIX ratio), SKEW index, put/call ratios, and realized-vs-implied volatility gaps. When options market pricing disagrees with consensus — for example, when consensus is bullish but term structure is inverted (VIX > VIX3M, signalling near-term fear) — we say so in the brief rather than forcing a synthetic consensus view. Options pricing often leads spot by 24 to 72 hours during stress events, so we treat it as a real-time polling mechanism, not a confirmation tool.

The deep-dive version of this framework, including current weightings, current quarter consensus reading, and our 2026 base-case derivation, is published separately at Our S&P 500 Forecast Methodology.

Forecast Output Convention

Every published forecast includes a base case (the most likely outcome), a bull case (an upside scenario with documented conditions that would have to hold), and a bear case (a downside scenario with named failure modes). The base case is not a single number — it is a range. We state the range, the underlying confidence interval, and the conditions that would invalidate the base case.

We do not use language that implies certainty we do not have. Our briefings say "our base case expects" and "data suggests" — not "the market will" or "we know." When the three pillars disagree, we publish the disagreement explicitly so the reader can see the underlying tension rather than a smoothed-over consensus view.

Data Sources & Verification

Every data point in every published piece cites its source. Primary-source preference is non-negotiable. We pull from:

  • CBOE — VIX spot, VIX3M, SKEW, put/call ratios, options market microstructure
  • FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data) — interest rates, yield curve, monetary aggregates, inflation series
  • BLS (Bureau of Labor Statistics) — CPI, PPI, nonfarm payrolls, employment, wage growth
  • SEC EDGAR — corporate filings, 10-K/10-Q/8-K material events, insider transactions
  • Treasury.gov — auction results, yield curve, debt issuance
  • Federal Reserve — FOMC statements, SEP projections, minutes, dot plots
  • CME FedWatch — implied probabilities for upcoming rate decisions

For broker-side data (real-time quotes, options chains, intraday price action), we use public.com as the live data source and cross-check against Yahoo Finance for verification when the data is critical to a published number. All data is current to the most recent available release — no stale statistics. If data is more than 30 days old at the time of publication, we note that in the affected paragraph.

Editorial Process

About 60% of our coverage is event-driven — earnings season, Federal Reserve meetings, CPI prints, jobs reports, geopolitical catalysts. The remaining 40% is editor-initiated: an evergreen piece on a specific strategy structure, a deep-dive on a particular volatility regime, or a synthesis of an emerging theme we have been tracking for several weeks. We do not write reviews or commentary based on tips, publicist pitches, or sample chapters.

Every article goes through a three-step review before publication: (1) the writing editor drafts the piece, (2) a second desk member reads and challenges the reasoning and the numbers, (3) the lead editor approves the final copy. For published forecasts that turn out wrong, we publish a post-mortem within 30 days describing what we got right, what we got wrong, and how the model will be adjusted.

Options Strategy Research Standards

Beyond market forecasting, we publish educational research on defined-risk options strategies — Iron Condors, Calendar Spreads, Bull Call Spreads, Covered Calls, Bull Put Spreads, and other structures that allow investors to express a market view with known, bounded risk. Every strategy guide covers entry criteria, position management, and exit rules.

We focus on strategies that survive the real world: wide-enough strikes to avoid premature assignment, position sizes calibrated to account for IV crush and gamma risk, and management rules that work across bull markets, bear markets, and range-bound periods alike. Every published position we hold in our own portfolio is logged in the public trade log with strike, expiry, entry credit/debit, and current mark.

Position Disclosure & Conflict Policy

Dependability Holdings LLC has no positions in any specific equity, ETF, or option mentioned in any article, commentary, or forecast unless explicitly disclosed at the time of publication. We do not hold positions ahead of publishing a directional view on the same instrument. When we publish a strategy guide and simultaneously hold that strategy in our own portfolio, the position is disclosed in the article body and in the trade log entry.

We are not affiliated with any broker-dealer, market maker, or issuer of securities discussed in our coverage. We accept no compensation from any security issuer. The site's only revenue is contextual advertising served through Google's AdSense program (full disclosure in our privacy policy) and any affiliate commissions explicitly tagged at the point of use.

What We Do Not Cover

Our coverage scope is intentionally narrow. We write about S&P 500 dynamics, VIX behavior, options strategies, macroeconomic indicators, and bond market dynamics. We do not cover climate change as a topic, do not frame investments through ESG or climate lenses, and do not include social-justice commentary in our analysis. We do not frame financial analysis through political lenses — we explain what policy is and what its documented effects have been, not whether it is good or bad. This is a scope statement, not a political statement. Our readers come to us for financial research, not for these topics, and we stay in our lane.
