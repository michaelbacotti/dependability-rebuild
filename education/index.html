Our education content is designed for traders who want to build genuine understanding rather than chase signals. Each piece explains the mechanics behind market phenomena, how options pricing works, and what institutional market participants are doing — and why. Sources include Cboe, OCC, SEC filings, and academic literature.

New education content is added regularly. A strong foundation in the material here makes our market commentary significantly more actionable.

Who this is for

The education section is built for readers who already have a basic working knowledge of options but want to deepen their understanding of how specific strategies behave under different market conditions. The content assumes the reader understands calls, puts, strike selection, and expiration mechanics. It does not assume the reader has traded options in a live account. Most pieces are written so a careful reader can understand the strategy well enough to evaluate whether it fits their own risk tolerance and time horizon — not so they can replicate the trades in our journal without doing their own analysis.

If you are new to options, start with the conceptual pieces in the strategy library (expected value, risk vs reward, time decay mechanics) before moving to the trade journal. The trade journal entries are dense with specific strikes, expirations, and pricing; they reward a reader who already has the conceptual scaffolding.

How the education section is organized

The content is organized into three buckets. The conceptual bucket covers the math and mechanics that underpin all options strategies: expected value, standard deviation of returns, probability of profit versus probability of being right, and the relationship between time decay and moneyness. The strategy bucket walks through specific structures — vertical spreads, iron condors, calendars, diagonals — with entry criteria, adjustment rules, and known failure modes for each. The case study bucket pulls specific trades from the journal and walks through the reasoning, entry decision, management decisions, and outcome in long-form prose.

Every piece in the education section is dated and tagged. The date matters because market conditions change — a bull put spread that worked in 2024 (a low-vol regime) may not work in 2026 (a higher-vol regime with different term structure dynamics). The tags let you filter by strategy, instrument, or market condition.

How the content is kept current

We update conceptual pieces when the underlying market structure changes in a way that makes the original framing misleading. For example, the expected value primer was updated in early 2026 when the CBOE changed its methodology for computing the expected move based on the VIX term structure. Strategy pieces are updated when we discover an adjustment rule that materially improves the outcome — the calendar spread primer was updated in mid-2025 after we observed that rolling at 21 DTE outperformed rolling at 14 DTE by ~3 percentage points of total return across 40+ trades.

We do not retire content that has aged out of usefulness. Instead, we add a header note explaining when the piece was last updated and what conditions it was written for. Readers can decide for themselves whether the framework still applies to current conditions.

Corrections and feedback

If you spot an error in a calculation, a typo in a formula, or a claim that does not match its cited source, please email edits@dependability.us. Verified corrections are appended to the bottom of the affected piece with a dated correction note. We aim to respond within two business days.
