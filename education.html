Options Education is the foundational layer of everything we publish at Dependability Holdings LLC. Our curriculum is built around a simple conviction: traders who understand the mechanics of what they're trading make better decisions than traders who are simply following signals or copying strategies they've seen elsewhere. If you are new to options, start here — not because the material is simple, but because building strong foundational models early prevents costly misunderstandings later. If you are an experienced trader who wants to tighten your mental models or fill gaps in your understanding of the Greeks, volatility pricing, or strategy construction, you will find something useful here as well.

The relationship between education and risk management is direct and inescapable. Every option position you take is essentially a bet on two simultaneous outcomes: where the underlying asset will move, and how the market will price the uncertainty around that movement. These two dimensions — directional risk and volatility risk — are managed separately in professional option desks, and understanding them as distinct forces is one of the first conceptual breakthroughs that separates informed traders from uninformed ones. When you understand how implied volatility is determined, how it responds to earnings announcements, and how vega — the Greek that measures volatility sensitivity — affects your position's value, you make dramatically different choices than a trader who simply buys calls because they are bullish. This is why our curriculum begins with the Greeks, not with strategies. The Greeks are not abstract mathematical concepts — they are the language of how options prices actually behave, and fluency in that language changes the quality of every decision you make.

Before you begin trading options, there are several things you should understand clearly about the instrument itself. Options are derivatives — their value is derived from the price of an underlying asset. That means option prices do not move independently; they respond to changes in the underlying, changes in time remaining until expiration, changes in implied volatility, and changes in interest rates. Each of these inputs is represented by a Greek letter, and each Greek has a directional meaning for your position: delta tells you how much the option gains or loses for a one-point move in the underlying, gamma tells you how fast that delta changes as the underlying moves, theta tells you how much value the option loses each day simply from time passing, and vega tells you how sensitive the option is to a one-point change in implied volatility. A trader who understands these four Greeks in combination has a far clearer picture of their risk than one who knows only the direction they expect the underlying to move.

Our curriculum covers three core areas. The first is foundational concepts — what options are, how they are priced, what determines their value, and the basic mechanics of calls and puts, including the difference between buying and selling, long and short positions, and the meaning of key terms like strike price, expiration date, premium, and in-the-money versus out-of-the-money. The second area is the Greeks in depth — not just the definitions, but the practical implications of each Greek for position management. Understanding delta means understanding probability and risk. Understanding gamma means understanding how your risk changes as the market moves. Understanding theta means understanding the cost of waiting and the value of time. Understanding vega means understanding how market fear and expectations are priced into options, which is especially critical around earnings season and macroeconomic announcements. The third area is strategy construction — how to build positions that express a specific market view while managing the risks that view entails.

Strategy education at Dependability covers both the mechanics and the risk profile of each approach. An iron condor looks simple on a diagram, but managing it requires understanding how the individual short options behave as expiration approaches, how gamma acceleration affects your P/L near expiration, and how to decide when to take profit or cut loss. A calendar spread is often described as a way to profit from time decay, but its actual risk-reward profile depends on the relationship between the near-term and longer-term options you are trading, and on the implied volatility of each. Bull call spreads, put spreads, straddles, strangles — each has a specific use case and a specific risk profile that our educational pieces explain in plain language. Glossary terms are linked inline throughout the curriculum so that you can build vocabulary naturally as you read, without needing to jump to a separate glossary page.

The key concepts we recommend mastering first, before moving to strategy-specific content, are: the meaning and behavior of delta and how it relates to probability; the difference between realized and implied volatility and why that difference matters; the mechanics of time decay and why theta is both an opportunity and a cost; the relationship between vega and the volatility surface; and the meaning of early exercise and assignment risk. These five concepts form the foundation on which all other knowledge builds. Without them, strategy content is just memorized rules. With them, strategy content becomes a framework you can reason about, adapt, and manage in real market conditions.
