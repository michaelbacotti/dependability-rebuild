BULL CASE RATIONALE

Why We're Bullish: A Late-May Market Assessment

Current Market Context

The S&P 500 closed at 7,473 on May 22, 2026 — up 0.9% for the week and marking the eighth straight winning week. This is a resilient market: it pulled back mid-week as the 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.6%+ (its highest in over a year) on renewed inflation fears tied to the Iran war and elevated oil prices, but buyers returned on Friday and pushed the index back to near its all-time high. The combination of strong corporate earnings (Ross Stores, Workday, Zoom all beat estimates this week), a Fed on hold, and stable if elevated volatility (VIX ~18–20) keeps the bull case intact.

The Earnings Engine

Technology and Communication Services continue to deliver double-digit earnings per share growth, driven by AI infrastructure buildout. The Magnificent Seven — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Tesla — have collectively grown EPS at a pace that justifies elevated multiples relative to historical averages. What has changed versus 12 months ago: the growth is beginning to broaden. Financials are inflecting as net interest income normalizes. Healthcare is showing margin improvement. Consumer discretionary is stabilizing. This week, Ross Stores, Workday, and Zoom beat profit expectations — additional data points confirming the broadening thesis. This broadening — if it persists into Q2 and Q3 earnings reports — reduces concentration risk and makes the market more durable.

The Federal Reserve and Discount Rates

Federal Reserve rate cuts — even if modest — reduce the discount rate applied to future earnings, mechanically increasing present value. The market currently prices in one to two cuts by year-end 2026. Each 25bp cut lifts equity fair value estimates by roughly 2–3% on a duration-weighted basis. The 10-year Treasury yield hit 4.6%+ this week — its highest level in over a year — as inflation fears mounted on the Iran conflict and elevated oil prices. This is a watch item: the 10Y has moved from the 4.2–4.6% range we cited last week into the upper end of that band. As long as it stays below 5%, valuation risk remains contained. If it breaks above 5%, the risk-of-contraction scenario from our Bear Case becomes relevant.

Seasonal and Structural Tailwinds

Since 1950, years ending in '6' have averaged +4.6% for the S&P 500 — a small but consistent historical edge. The May–July window has historically been favorable for risk assets, particularly when the prior year closed positive (2025 did). The eighth straight winning week is a testament to the seasonal tailwind in action. Post-election years (2025 was one) tend to see reduced policy uncertainty in the second half, allowing capital expenditure planning to accelerate — and AI capex is the dominant form of that spending right now. Consumer sentiment data released this week hit a record low on inflation concerns — a cautionary signal, but one that has not yet manifested in hard spending data.

Our Specific Targets and the Path There

1-Month Target: 7,600 (+1.7%). This reflects a modest continuation of the May recovery trend. SPX closed Friday at 7,473, within striking distance of the all-time high. Benign macro data and more earnings beats should push toward 7,600 over the next four weeks.

3-Month Target: 7,700 (+3.0%). This level is where the bull case thesis gains structural confirmation. If Q2 earnings reports (reporting in July–August) show breadth improvement — non-AI sectors contributing positively — the market can absorb the concentrated AI valuations and push to this level. A VIX drop below 17 would be a confirming signal. The 10Y stabilizing below 4.7% would also remove the primary near-term risk.

Year-End 2026 Base Case: 7,700 (+3.0%). More conservative than 7,800 because the path is not linear. Late-year seasonal weakness, potential Fed missteps, or geopolitical shocks could create drawdowns along the way. The Iran war dynamic — and its impact on oil and inflation — is the single most watched geopolitical variable. We model 7,700 as the most probable year-end close given the current setup.

Bull Case (Stretch): 8,000. Requires an AI productivity breakout visible in Q2/Q3 earnings revisions, Fed cutting earlier than currently priced (first cut by July), and no geopolitical disruption. This is the scenario where everything goes right — earnings growth accelerates, breadth broadens, and multiple expansion accompanies it.

Risks to the Thesis

Three scenarios could push targets lower: (1) Hard landing — unemployment spikes above 5.5%, GDP turns negative, the Fed is forced to cut aggressively but earnings estimates fall faster — base case drops to 6,800–7,000. (2) Geopolitical shock — oil spike above $120 on Iran escalation, VIX re-tests 30+, risk-off contagion — targets reset to 6,500–7,000. (3) Valuation contraction — 10-year Treasury yield breaks above 5% on renewed inflation fear — multiple compression removes 10–15% from fair value regardless of earnings. These scenarios are not our base case, but they are not tail risk either.

Bottom line: The S&P 500 at 7,473 is not expensive relative to a projected ~$260 in 2026 EPS (≈ 28.7x forward P/E, consistent with where the market has traded during this AI investment cycle). Our targets reflect a market that has room to grow if the earnings broadening story proves real, with 7,700 as the most probable year-end outcome. The yield spike this week is a warning flag — not a trigger — and one we are watching closely heading into next week.

Our Methodology

We build our S&P 500 targets by synthesizing three inputs: (1) Wall Street consensus — the average of major institutional price targets from Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, UBS, HSBC, and JPMorgan; (2) historical seasonality — the S&P 500 has averaged +4.6% in years ending in '6' since 1950, and the May–July window has historically been favorable; (3) options market signals — the VIX term structure and implied move data tell us how much uncertainty the market is pricing near-term.

We weight these inputs by track record and relevance: bank consensus gets the most weight because it reflects fundamental analysis by dedicated equity strategy teams; seasonal patterns inform timing; implied vol calibrates our confidence interval. The result is a base case (most likely), a bull case (upside scenario), and a bear case (downside scenario) — not a single number that hides uncertainty.
