BULL CASE RATIONALE

Why We're Bullish: A Mid-May Market Assessment

Current Market Context

The S&P 500 closed at 7,501 on May 14, 2026 — within 2% of its all-time high. This is not a market that is ebullient or frothy; it is a market digesting a powerful AI investment cycle while inflation cools and the Federal Reserve signals a path toward policy normalization. The combination of elevated but stabilizing volatility (VIX ~18–20), solid earnings growth, and a Fed on hold creates a constructive backdrop for equities over the next six months.

The Earnings Engine

Technology and Communication Services continue to deliver double-digit earnings per share growth, driven by AI infrastructure buildout. The Magnificent Seven — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Tesla — have collectively grown EPS at a pace that justifies elevated multiples relative to historical averages. What has changed versus 12 months ago: the growth is beginning to broaden. Financials are inflecting as net interest income normalizes. Healthcare is showing margin improvement. Consumer discretionary is stabilizing. This broadening — if it persists into Q2 and Q3 earnings reports — reduces concentration risk and makes the market more durable.

The Federal Reserve and Discount Rates

Federal Reserve rate cuts — even if modest — reduce the discount rate applied to future earnings, mechanically increasing present value. The market currently prices in one to two cuts by year-end 2026. Each 25bp cut lifts equity fair value estimates by roughly 2–3% on a duration-weighted basis. The 10-year Treasury yield has stabilized in the 4.2–4.6% range, removing the multiple compression threat that weighed on markets in 2024. As long as the 10Y stays below 5%, valuation risk remains contained.

Seasonal and Structural Tailwinds

Since 1950, years ending in '6' have averaged +4.6% for the S&P 500 — a small but consistent historical edge. The May–July window has historically been favorable for risk assets, particularly when the prior year closed positive (2025 did). Post-election years (2025 was one) tend to see reduced policy uncertainty in the second half, allowing capital expenditure planning to accelerate — and AI capex is the dominant form of that spending right now.

Our Specific Targets and the Path There

1-Month Target: 7,650 (+2.0%). This requires a modest rally following the May 14 close. Earnings momentum and benign macro data should be sufficient to push SPX toward this level over the next four weeks.

3-Month Target: 7,800 (+4.0%). This is the level where our bull case thesis gains confirmation. If Q2 earnings reports (reporting in July–August) show breadth improvement — non-AI sectors contributing positively — the market can absorb the concentrated AI valuations and push to this level. The VIX dropping below 17 would be a confirming signal.

Year-End 2026 Base Case: 7,700 (+2.7%). More conservative than 7,800 because the path is not linear. Late-year seasonal weakness, potential Fed missteps, or geopolitical shocks could create drawdowns along the way. We model 7,700 as the most probable year-end close given the current setup.

Bull Case (Stretch): 8,000. Requires an AI productivity breakout visible in Q2/Q3 earnings revisions, Fed cutting earlier than currently priced (first cut by July), and no geopolitical disruption. This is the scenario where everything goes right — earnings growth accelerates, breadth broadens, and multiple expansion accompanies it.

Risks to the Thesis

Three scenarios could push targets lower: (1) Hard landing — unemployment spikes above 5.5%, GDP turns negative, the Fed is forced to cut aggressively but earnings estimates fall faster — base case drops to 6,800–7,000. (2) Geopolitical shock — oil spike above $120, VIX re-tests 30+, risk-off contagion — targets reset to 6,500–7,000. (3) Valuation contraction — 10-year Treasury yield breaks above 5% on renewed inflation fear — multiple compression removes 10–15% from fair value regardless of earnings. These scenarios are not our base case, but they are not tail risk either.

Bottom line: The S&P 500 at 7,501 is not expensive relative to a projected ~$260 in 2026 EPS (≈ 28.8x forward P/E, consistent with where the market has traded during this AI investment cycle). Our targets reflect a market that has room to grow if the earnings broadening story proves real, with 7,700 as the most probable year-end outcome.

Our Methodology

We build our S&P 500 targets by synthesizing three inputs: (1) Wall Street consensus — the average of major institutional price targets from Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, UBS, HSBC, and JPMorgan; (2) historical seasonality — the S&P 500 has averaged +4.6% in years ending in '6' since 1950, and the May–July window has historically been favorable; (3) options market signals — the VIX term structure and implied move data tell us how much uncertainty the market is pricing near-term.

We weight these inputs by track record and relevance: bank consensus gets the most weight because it reflects fundamental analysis by dedicated equity strategy teams; seasonal patterns inform timing; implied vol calibrates our confidence interval. The result is a base case (most likely), a bull case (upside scenario), and a bear case (downside scenario) — not a single number that hides uncertainty.
