Types of Investment Risk

Understanding the various categories of investment risk is essential before engaging with any of the content on this site. Market risk refers to the possibility that an entire market or asset class declines in value, affecting most investments within that category. Credit risk arises when a borrower fails to meet a financial obligation, potentially impacting bondholders and other creditors. Liquidity risk describes the danger that an investor may not be able to buy or sell an asset quickly enough at a fair price, particularly relevant in less liquid markets or during periods of market stress. Interest rate risk affects the value of fixed-income instruments when prevailing rates change. Currency risk emerges when investments are denominated in foreign currencies subject to exchange rate fluctuations. Concentration risk occurs when a portfolio is heavily weighted in a single asset, sector, or geographic region, amplifying the impact of any adverse developments in that area. Each of these risk categories can affect options strategies in specific ways, and our educational content explores these relationships in detail.

Options-Specific Risks

Options trading involves a set of risks that are distinct from those associated with traditional equity or bond investing, and readers should fully understand these risks before engaging with any options-related content on this site.

Premium loss risk: When you purchase an option, the premium you pay represents the maximum potential loss on that position. However, if the option expires worthless — which happens frequently for option buyers, especially in low-volatility environments — the entire premium is lost. This is not a rare occurrence; studies of retail options flow consistently show that a significant majority of purchased options expire worthless, which is why premium sellers have a statistical edge over time.

Unlimited loss potential on naked writes: Selling a naked call option exposes the seller to theoretically unlimited loss potential, as the underlying stock has no upper bound on how high it can climb. Similarly, selling naked puts creates substantial downside exposure, effectively equivalent to purchasing the underlying at the strike price while receiving only a limited premium.

Leverage and margin risk: Options positions are often established with margin, which amplifies both gains and losses. A small adverse move in the underlying can result in a margin call that forces the liquidation of positions at unfavorable times. The use of leverage in options strategies — whether through notional value comparisons or direct margin borrowing — requires careful position sizing and risk management discipline.

Volatility risk: Implied volatility is a key determinant of option prices, and unexpected moves in volatility — either a spike or a collapse — can dramatically affect the value of option positions regardless of the underlying's directional movement. A position that appears well-structured based on current volatility levels can quickly become unprofitable if implied volatility changes sharply, which often happens around earnings announcements, economic data releases, and geopolitical events.

Early assignment risk: Owners of options may exercise them at any time before expiration, and short option positions are subject to assignment at any time, including at inopportune moments. Short call positions, in particular, are subject to assignment if the underlying pays a dividend and the option goes in-the-money. Unexpected assignment can create sudden, significant margin pressure and disrupt carefully planned position management.

Gap risk: Options positions can be severely impacted by overnight or weekend gaps in the underlying's price. A position that appeared to have adequate protective buffer based on the prior day's close may be dramatically underwater following an overnight announcement, geopolitical development, or macroeconomic surprise.

Past Performance Limitations

Past performance is not indicative of future results. This statement is not a legal formality — it reflects a fundamental truth about financial markets. The financial markets are inherently unpredictable, and any historical performance data, backtested results, or theoretical projections presented on this site are for illustrative purposes only. Backtesting, in particular, is subject to significant limitations that are important to understand. Backtested results typically assume perfect execution at hypothetical prices, ignoring real-world factors such as slippage, bid-ask spreads, and liquidity constraints. They also assume the ability to hold positions through all market conditions without behavioral interference, which rarely reflects actual investor behavior. Furthermore, markets adapt to successful strategies — a pattern that worked reliably in the past may cease to work as more participants attempt to exploit it, changing the very dynamics that made it effective. Any historical results cited on this site should be evaluated with these limitations clearly in mind, and no investment decision should be made based primarily on historical performance data.
