Daily Move: Thursday, June 18 — Post-FOMC Recovery, Tech Leads, Energy Lags

As of Thursday, June 18, 2026 (4:00 PM ET close, public.com realtime), the S&P 500 closed at 7,500.58 — up +1.08% on the session and +1.44% week-to-date — as the market shrugged off Wednesday's hawkish Federal Reserve surprise. The VIX held at 18.44 (Wednesday's close, the print from the FOMC day itself), the 10-year Treasury yield was at 4.49% (Tuesday's print; yfinance 10Y data lags), and oil settled near $76.79. The S&P recovery was led by technology (XLK +1.89%) and consumer discretionary (XLY +2.60%), with financials (XLF +0.35%) and healthcare (XLV +0.25%) up modestly. The day's laggards were communication services, energy, and utilities. The market is closed tomorrow (Friday, June 19) for Juneteenth; the next session is Monday, June 22.

What Drove the Tape

Wednesday's FOMC was the dominant story, and Thursday is the reaction. The June 16–17 FOMC meeting — the first under new Chair Kevin Warsh, sworn in May 22, 2026 — held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% by a unanimous 12-0 vote. The vote was widely expected (97% probability per CME FedWatch on June 13). The real news was in the Summary of Economic Projections: the median 2026 federal funds rate projection flipped from 3.4% in March to 3.8% in June, moving from an implied cut to an implied hike above today's midpoint of ~3.625%. Of 18 officials, 9 now project rates higher by year-end (8 at midpoint, 1 below). Seventeen of eighteen officials see inflation risks tilted to the upside. The Fed raised its 2026 PCE inflation forecast to 3.6% from 2.7%. The 2-year Treasury yield jumped ~11bp to ~4.15% on the day; the 10-year rose ~4bp to ~4.47%.

The equity reaction Wednesday was sharp: the S&P 500 closed at 7,420.10, down -1.21% on the session, with the VIX spiking +12.4% to 18.44. The market read the dot plot as the end of the "easing bias" the June statement explicitly dropped.

Thursday's recovery is the equity market saying: hawkish path is real, but the underlying earnings and growth thesis is intact. Tech rebounded 1.9% — leadership from semiconductors and AI infrastructure absorbed the higher-for-longer path because their growth is structural, not rate-sensitive. Consumer discretionary (+2.6%) caught a tailwind from the US–Iran interim peace agreement (oil down ~12% WTD, easing inflation pressure on the consumer). Energy lagged as oil's decline hit XLE (-0.37% on the day, -4.64% WTD). Defensive sectors (XLU, XLP) were modestly mixed as the market priced in "higher rates for longer but no immediate recession." Industrials (XLI) underperformed on no sector-specific catalyst; the public.com quote (-5.32% from Wednesday's yfinance close) is post-market and may include a single off-exchange print — the bid ($179.78) and ask ($184.00) are far tighter than the "last" of $170.29.

Tech + cyclicals absorbed the hawkish flip. XLK (+1.89%) and XLY (+2.60%) led Thursday, with semiconductor and consumer-discretionary strength doing the heavy lifting. The thesis is straightforward: the dot plot's hike path raises the discount rate, but it does not change the structural earnings trajectory for AI infrastructure or the consumer's underlying spending power. The 1.9% tech rebound is a quality bid — investors rotated within the leadership group rather than out of it. The risk: a hotter-than-expected Core PCE print or a re-acceleration in jobless claims would test this thesis immediately.

Energy is the WTD laggard by a wide margin. XLE -4.64% WTD is the single biggest move in the sector complex, almost entirely explained by crude's -12% WTD drop on the US–Iran interim peace agreement. With oil now in the $76 area and the geopolitical premium largely extracted, the sector trades as a pure oil-price proxy. Watch for a base if crude holds the $75 level; otherwise, a retest of $70 oil would drag XLE toward the $52 area. The structural long thesis (underinvestment in upstream capex) is intact but is a 2-3 quarter story, not a 2-3 week story.

Financials benefited from the steeper curve. A hawkish dot plot with a steeper path typically helps the banks via net interest margin upside. XLF is up +3.08% WTD despite Thursday's modest +0.35% print, with money-center banks (JPM, BAC, WFC) participating in the move. The sector is the cleanest "hawkish Fed" beneficiary in the equity market right now. Position sizing should favor the diversified money-centers over the rate-sensitive regionals, where deposit beta is a real headwind.

Defensives faded on the post-FOMC risk-on rotation. XLV, XLP, and XLU were all modestly green on Thursday but negative on the week. The pattern: the defensive bid that materialized during Wednesday's FOMC shock unwound on Thursday's recovery. With the VIX settling at 18.44 (well below the 1-year average of ~20) and no imminent recession signal, the rotation out of defensives is more positioning-driven than thesis-driven. Healthcare remains the structural overweight on demographic tailwinds; the WTD underperformance is a setup, not a signal.

Industrials show a data integrity issue, not a thesis break. XLI's public.com "last" of $170.29 appears to be a post-market off-exchange print — the bid ($179.78) and ask ($184.00) are far tighter, and the implied move from Wednesday's yfinance close is implausible. Using the bid as a more reliable intraday reference, XLI is approximately flat on the day (-0.10%), not -5.32%. The Thursday weakness on a clean read would have been modest; the WTD -2.77% is the real number to track. This will be verified at Monday's open.
