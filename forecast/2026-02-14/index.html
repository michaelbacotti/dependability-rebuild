S&P 500 Forecast — February 14, 2026

Bull Case Rationale

Year-of-the-dog historical pattern: Since 1950, years ending in '6' have averaged +4.6% for the S&P 500. February marks the start of the seasonally strongest mid-year window.

AI capex acceleration post-DeepSeek pivot: The January 2026 DeepSeek breakthrough forced a reassessment of AI infrastructure efficiency — capital deployment accelerated rather than contracted, as firms moved from "do more with less" to "deploy at scale." Nvidia and the hyperscalers confirmed Q4 2025 capex figures well above estimates.

Fed rate cut expectations: Markets began pricing in Fed cuts by mid-2026 following weak January jobs data. Even one or two cuts reduce the discount rate applied to future earnings, mechanically lifting equity valuations.

Targets

1-Month: 7,400 (+0.8%). Modest continuation of the February recovery trend.

3-Month: 7,500 (+2.2%). Q1 earnings season (reporting March–April) as the next catalyst.

Year-End 2026: 7,600 (+3.5%). Base case reflecting AI capex cycle extending into H2.

Bull Case: 7,800. Requires visible AI productivity gains in Q1 earnings, Fed cutting by June, and no geopolitical disruption.

Risks

A hard landing (unemployment spikes, GDP negative), geopolitical shock (oil spike on Iran escalation), or valuation contraction (10-year breaks above 5%) could push base case to 6,800–7,000.

Our Methodology

We build our S&P 500 targets by synthesizing three inputs: (1) Wall Street consensus — the average of major institutional price targets from Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, UBS, HSBC, and JPMorgan; (2) historical seasonality — the S&P 500 has averaged +4.6% in years ending in '6' since 1950; (3) options market signals — the VIX term structure and implied move data tell us how much uncertainty the market is pricing near-term.

We weight these inputs by track record and relevance: bank consensus gets the most weight because it reflects fundamental analysis by dedicated equity strategy teams; seasonal patterns inform timing; implied vol calibrates our confidence interval.
