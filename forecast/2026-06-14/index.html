BULL CASE RATIONALE

The Week the Bears Folded: CPI Reset and the Path to New Highs

As of Friday, June 12, 2026, the S&P 500 closed at 7,431.46 — a modest +0.4% gain on the week that masked an extraordinary intraday reversal. Tuesday's intraday low of 7,237.85 (a 3.3% drawdown from the prior Friday's 7,484) was a textbook overreaction to pre-CPI anxiety, fully recovered by Friday's close just five sessions later. The week was the market's most efficient correction of 2026: sharp on the way down, equally sharp on the way up, ending with the index roughly flat and the VIX back below the 1-year mean.

The technical reset is complete. From the midweek low of 7,265.93 (Wednesday's intraday low after the CPI report was digested), the S&P rallied +2.3% to 7,431 by Friday. RSI oscillated from a brief oversold reading on Wednesday back to neutral (~52-55) by the close. There is no technical overhang, no breakout, no breakdown — the tape absorbed the inflation scare and reset the path for the FOMC.

The VIX collapse is the data point that matters most for the week ahead. Volatility opened Monday at 20.29, spiked to 22.22 on Wednesday's close (the day the May CPI was released), then collapsed to 17.68 by Friday — a roughly 20% decline in two sessions and the first sub-1-year-mean close since mid-May. The options market is signaling that the inflation scare is over and that the upcoming FOMC is fully priced for a hold. Implied moves have compressed to roughly ±1.5% weekly.

Oil's -7% drop is the most important macro signal. WTI crude fell from $91.30 (prior Friday close) to $84.88 — a $6.42 weekly decline. This is a geopolitical disinflation signal, not a demand-destruction signal: the Iran-ceasefire premium that lifted oil to $91+ in late May and early June is fading, easing near-term inflation pressure. Lower input costs (airlines, manufacturers, chemicals, freight) are a direct earnings tailwind.

Breadth was constructive, not defensive. Small caps outperformed the large-cap index (Russell 2000 +3.1% on the week), cyclicals and materials led the recovery (XLB +4.4%, XLI +1.5%), and the rate-sensitive sectors benefited from a 6 bp decline in the 10-year yield to 4.49%. This is the kind of breadth pattern that historically precedes a continuation of the uptrend, not a regime change.

What Others Are Missing: Falling Oil Is Bullish, Not Bearish

The reflexive narrative around oil's decline is: "Falling oil = demand destruction = economic slowdown = bear market." That is the wrong read, and it is costing investors positioning.

The oil decline is a geopolitical disinflation signal. When geopolitical tail risks (Iran escalation, Red Sea disruption) fade, oil reverts toward fundamental supply-demand equilibrium. The $91+ oil in late May and early June was elevated by an $8-12/bbl risk premium — that premium is now coming out. At $84.88, oil is closer to fair value given current global demand. Lower input costs for airlines, manufacturers, chemical companies, and transportation firms are a direct earnings tailwind.

The disinflation story is back on track. The May CPI print (+0.1% headline, +0.2% core) confirms that the inflation scare from the April print (+0.5% core) was an anomaly, not a trend. Goods deflation is back — and goods deflation is what the Fed needed to see to justify "higher for longer" without actually hiking. The market now prices roughly zero 2026 cuts, but a soft CPI gives the Fed room to keep one cut on the table in the December dot plot without changing their reaction function.

Tech's midweek capitulation was a multiple-compression event, not a fundamentals event. XLK traded as low as 172.88 intraday Tuesday before recovering to 184.80 by Friday — a 6.8% intraday drawdown that was fully retraced. The Q2 earnings preannouncement window opens in late June; until then, the week's volatility was positioning-driven, not fundamentals-driven. AI capex from hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) is widely tracked at $300+ billion for 2026, and that figure has not changed.

The 10-year yield at 4.49% is lower than where it closed last Friday (4.55%) — despite a week of inflation fears. This is the bond market's verdict: the inflation scare is temporary, and the long end is refusing to re-price higher. A stable or declining 10-year yield is a tailwind for tech multiples, which is exactly what we saw Friday (QQQ +0.6%, XLK recovering into the close). The correlation between rates and tech is reasserting itself: lower rates = higher multiples.

This is the kind of week that builds the foundation for the year-end target. Soft CPI → disinflation narrative restored → Fed has optionality → bond yields stable → tech multiples expand → S&P grinds higher. The correction was a 5-day noise event in a 12-month uptrend.

The Earnings Engine: Q2 Preannouncement Window Opens Late June

Q2 2026 earnings season does not begin in earnest until mid-July, but the preannouncement window opens in late June — and this is the key risk event for the week of June 15-19.

Q1 2026 was strong. Approximately 80% of S&P 500 companies that reported by mid-May beat EPS estimates. The Magnificent Seven collectively grew earnings 20%+ year-over-year. The question for Q2 is whether the broadening continues: industrials, financials, healthcare, and consumer discretionary all need to show that AI-driven productivity gains are spreading beyond the technology sector.

The semiconductor preannouncement risk is real but limited. Last week's intraday low in XLK (172.88 Tuesday) was a positioning event, not a fundamentals event. The actual Q2 earnings preannouncements from semiconductor equipment companies (Applied Materials, KLA, Lam Research) would be the real signal. Our base case: capex remains elevated, and any guidance that implies a Q2 slowdown would be a surprise.

Watch for consumer guidance in late June. The May retail sales report (Tuesday June 17, same day as FOMC begins) is the consumer health check. Strong retail sales + soft CPI = the Goldilocks scenario that sends the S&P to 7,600+ by end of July.

The Federal Reserve: June 16–17 FOMC — The Most Important Meeting of the Year

The FOMC meets Tuesday-Wednesday June 16-17, with the rate decision and statement released at 2:00 p.m. ET on Wednesday June 17. This is the dominant catalyst for the week ahead.

The Fed will hold rates steady at 4.25-4.50%. This is fully priced. The market is not expecting a cut. The question is what the updated dot plot says about 2026.

The dot plot is the key. The March 2026 dot plot showed two 2026 cuts. The June 2026 dot plot will be updated to reflect: (1) the May jobs report (+172k, stronger than expected), (2) the May CPI print (+0.1% headline, softer than feared), and (3) the oil decline (disinflation signal). Our base case: the dot plot is revised to show one cut in 2026 (most likely December), with the other cut pushed to 2027. A "one cut in 2026" dot plot would be slightly hawkish relative to current market pricing (zero cuts), but would signal that the Fed is still on a path toward normalization — which is constructive for risk assets.

The statement and press conference are more important than the decision itself. Watch for: (1) any language change around "confidence" in inflation returning to 2%, (2) any acknowledgment that oil's decline has eased near-term inflation pressure, and (3) any shift in the characterization of the labor market from "solid" to "moderating." A statement that acknowledges disinflation progress without signaling imminent cuts is the most likely outcome — and would be net neutral to slightly bullish.

The pre-meeting blackout began June 13. Fed speakers are not making public comments in the days leading into the meeting. The week of June 8-12 was the final window for Fed speakers to shape expectations. There were no major hawkish surprises from Fed speakers this week — a relief.

Risks to the Thesis

(1) Hawkish FOMC on June 17. A dot plot signaling zero 2026 cuts with hikes priced would be a regime change. The 10-year yield would reprice above 4.75%, multiple compression resumes, and the S&P could test 7,100-7,200. Watch the dot plot carefully.

(2) Q2 earnings preannouncement weakness. Preannouncements begin in late June. Any major company guiding down on revenue or margin would confirm that the strong labor market isn't translating into corporate revenue strength — a stagflationary signal.

(3) AI capex moderation. If hyperscalers guide to lower 2026 capex in Q2 calls (mid-July), the tech selloff extends. Our base case: capex remains at $300+ billion, but the risk is real.

(4) Iran deal collapses. If ceasefire talks fail, oil spikes back to $95-100 and the inflation narrative becomes entrenched. Counterintuitively, that would be bullish for the dollar and bearish for bonds and equities simultaneously.

(5) Geopolitical tail risk. A credit event, a major bank failure, or a sovereign debt crisis would compound the multiple compression.

(6) Consumer slowdown. May retail sales (Tuesday June 17) is the consumer health check. Weak retail sales + strong jobs = stagflationary signal that the Fed cannot cut and the consumer is weakening.

How We Forecast

We build our S&P 500 targets by synthesizing three inputs: (1) Wall Street consensus — the average of major institutional price targets from Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, JPMorgan, and UBS; (2) historical seasonality — the June-August window has historically been favorable for equities, and years ending in '6' have averaged +4.6% for the S&P 500 since 1950; (3) options market signals — VIX at 17.68 is now below its 1-year mean, signaling that the inflation scare has passed and implied moves have compressed to roughly ±1.5% weekly.

We weight these inputs by track record and relevance: bank consensus gets the most weight because it reflects fundamental analysis by dedicated equity strategy teams; seasonal patterns inform timing; implied vol calibrates our confidence interval and warns us when the market is pricing extreme outcomes (in either direction). After this week's CPI print and VIX collapse, the inputs are converging: consensus sits at 7,825 (+5.3% from current), seasonality is turning favorable, and the volatility complex is signaling lower risk. We are constructive on the 3-12 month horizon.
