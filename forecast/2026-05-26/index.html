BULL CASE RATIONALE

The Moon as Economic Frontier: A Late-May Market Assessment

Current Situation

The S&P 500 closed at 7,508 on May 26, 2026 — up modestly from Friday's 7,473 as markets digest a week that saw the 10-year Treasury yield touch 4.7% on Iran-related energy concerns before settling lower. It has been eight straight winning weeks. Volatility has normalized (VIX ~17), the Fed remains on hold, and corporate earnings continue to come in ahead of depressed expectations. The bull case is intact — but it is no longer cheap, and the easy gains are behind us.

Today, President Trump announced the Artemis Accords 2.0 — an expanded framework for commercial lunar development that includes property rights for mining claims, tax incentives for launch providers, and a formal NASA-corporate partnership structure for a permanent crewed Moon base by 2030. The announcement has already moved space stocks and is worth examining as both a geopolitical signal and an investment thesis.

The Earnings Engine: Broadening Still In Progress

Technology and Communication Services continue to deliver double-digit earnings growth. But the broadening thesis — financial sector inflecting on net interest income normalization, healthcare showing margin improvement, consumer discretionary stabilizing — is starting to show up in the data. Ross Stores, Workday, and Zoom beat estimates last week. This week the focus shifts to retail and homebuilder sentiment data.

The risk: if Q2 earnings season ends with growth still concentrated in the Mag Seven, the multiple re-rating story stalls. Watch for equal-weight S&P 500 performance versus cap-weight performance over the next 60 days — if equal-weight starts outperforming, the broadening trade is real and the market can sustain higher multiples without a correction.

The Federal Reserve and the Discount Rate Question

The Fed is data-dependent and on hold. The 10-year Treasury at 4.5–4.7% reflects moderate inflation anxiety, not the 5%+ level that would mechanically compress equity multiples. Each 25bp rate cut that gets priced in lifts equity fair value by roughly 2–3% on a duration-weighted basis. The market currently prices in one to two cuts by year-end 2026. If the Iran situation stabilizes and oil pullbacks from current levels, that pricing could extend — supporting equities in the near term.

The risk: if oil stays elevated ($90+) through Q2, the inflation narrative regains traction and the 10Y tests 5%. That is the scenario where valuation compression removes 10–15% from fair value regardless of earnings.

Seasonal and Structural Tailwinds

Years ending in '6' have averaged +4.6% for the S&P 500 since 1950. We are in the May–July window that has historically been favorable for risk assets, particularly when the prior year closed positive (2025 did). The eighth straight winning week is remarkable but not unprecedented in strong bull markets — it typically precedes continued strength rather than mean-reversion.

The space economy announcement today is a microcosm of the structural thesis: U.S. fiscal and industrial policy is allocating structural capital to emerging sectors (AI infrastructure, space, advanced manufacturing) to replace the productivity gap left by declining fiscal stimulus. That capital allocation is showing up in capex numbers. It will show up in earnings revisions over the next 12–18 months.

S&P 500 Sector Breakdown

The S&P 500 is weighted across 11 primary sectors. Here is how each is performing as of late May 2026:

Technology (~29% of index): The largest sector by weight remains the primary earnings engine. AI infrastructure spending (Nvidia, AMD, custom silicon at Google/Microsoft/Amazon) continues to drive double-digit revenue and earnings growth for the semiconductor supply chain. Valuation is elevated — the sector trades at ~30x forward earnings — but growth justifies it if the AI capex cycle continues. Watch for Q2 guidance to confirm the trajectory.

Healthcare (~12.5% of index): Biotech and medtech have recovered from the 2024-25 pricing pressure. Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk continue to dominate the GLP-1 market, which is expanding into obesity, cardiovascular risk reduction, and sleep apnea. UnitedHealth's margins have stabilized after the prior-year pricing scrutiny. Healthcare is no longer a headwind for broad index earnings — it is a contributor.

Financials (~13% of index): The banking sector is in an inflection point. Net interest income has normalized upward as short-term rates stayed elevated while long-term rates climbed. JPMorgan, Bank of America, and Goldman Sachs all reported better-than-expected NII in Q1. Commercial real estate credit concerns have not resolved, but the large money-center banks have built adequate reserves. If the 10Y stabilizes below 4.7%, financials are the sector most likely to outperform consensus expectations in Q2.

Consumer Discretionary (~10.5% of index): Consumer spending is mixed. Amazon and Home Depot both gave cautious guidance at their last earnings — Amazon citing macro uncertainty, Home Depot citing continued deferral of large DIY projects. The lower end of consumer spending (Ross Stores, TJX) is holding up well; the middle market is under pressure. Luxury (LVMH, Kering) is struggling. Watch May retail sales data and homebuilder sentiment this week — both will clarify whether the consumer is holding or weakening.

Communication Services (~8.5% of index): Alphabet and Meta continue to compound at 15-20% revenue growth, driven by AI-powered ad targeting improvements. Both are deploying significant capex for AI infrastructure. Netflix's streaming ad tier is gaining traction. The sector's earnings quality is high, but concentration risk in the Mag Seven means sector-level volatility remains elevated.

Industrials (~8% of index): This is the sector to watch in 2026. The Moon base announcement and infrastructure bill capex are flowing into defense contractors (Lockheed, Raytheon, Northrop), aerospace (Boeing, Spirit Aero), and construction/engineering names (Jacobs, AECOM). The Iran conflict has also driven a defense spending tailwind. This sector is experiencing its most sustained earnings acceleration since the 2018-2019 period. Aerospace and defense names may be the best relative value in the market right now.

Consumer Staples (~6% of index): Steady, defensive, uninspiring. Procter & Gamble and Coca-Cola are raising prices modestly and seeing volume hold. This sector does not lead bull markets but provides stability in drawdowns. Not a overweight at current valuations (the sector trades at ~24x forward earnings in a market that has other opportunities).

Energy (~4.5% of index): Oil has been elevated by Iran conflict supply concerns. Exxon and Chevron are generating significant free cash flow at $85-$90 oil. The risk: if the Iran situation de-escalates, oil could retreat to $70-$75, which would compress energy sector earnings and create a headwind for the index in the near term. The Moon base announcement includes in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) — using lunar water ice for rocket propellant — which is a long-dated threat to terrestrial oil demand. Energy is a tactical trade, not a structural overweight.

Utilities (~2% of index): Utilities have been pressured by the 10Y rise — utility valuations are sensitive to long rates. Data center power demand (AI compute infrastructure) is creating a new utility narrative that is partially offsetting the rate headwind. Not a core position at current yields.

Real Estate (~2.5% of index): Commercial real estate (office in particular) remains under pressure. Data center and industrial REITs are performing well. The broader sector is not a contributor to index-level earnings growth in 2026.

Materials (~2.5% of index): Cement, steel, and specialty chemicals are tied to infrastructure spending and domestic manufacturing (the "onshoring" theme). The Moon base announcement includes in-situ manufacturing using lunar materials — again, a long-dated story. Copper demand from AI data centers and EV charging infrastructure is a real tailwind. Materials is the most levered sector to the infrastructure/space investment thesis short-term.

  • Wednesday: EIA petroleum status report — Iran-related supply headlines will move energy names and the broader market if oil spikes
  • Thursday: Weekly jobless claims — labor market resilience is the linchpin of the soft-landing thesis; any deterioration would be a negative signal
  • Friday: Flash PMI (Manufacturing and Services, June) — early-month economic data to calibrate Q2 trajectory

Our Specific Targets

1-Month Target: 7,600 (+1.2%). SPX closed at 7,508 today. Benign data and continued earnings beats should push toward 7,600 over the next four weeks. The Moon base news adds a modest aerospace/defense premium to sentiment.

3-Month Target: 7,750 (+3.2%). This level is where the bull case needs to prove itself. Q2 earnings reports (July–August) must show breadth improvement — non-AI sectors contributing positively. If the equal-weight S&P outperforms, the market can absorb AI concentrations and push here. A VIX drop below 16 would be confirming.

Year-End 2026 Base Case: 7,800 (+3.9%). Reflects a market that grows into its elevated multiple as earnings broaden. The Iran conflict and oil price trajectory remain the single most important near-term variable. Year-end 7,800 is achievable if geopolitical risk remains contained and the Fed gets one cut.

Bull Case (Stretch): 8,200. Requires visible AI productivity breakout in Q2/Q3 earnings revisions, the Fed cutting earlier than currently priced (first cut by September), oil retreating below $80, AND the space/industrial new economy thesis gaining institutional traction. High bar, but not implausible.

Risks to the Thesis

(1) Hard landing — unemployment spikes above 5.5%, GDP turns negative. Base case drops to 6,800–7,000. (2) Geopolitical shock — oil spike above $100 on Iran escalation, VIX re-tests 30+. Targets reset to 6,500–7,000. (3) Valuation contraction — 10-year breaks above 5% on inflation re-acceleration. Multiple compression removes 10–15% from fair value regardless of earnings. (4) Space bubble — if the Moon base narrative overextends into pure speculation (valuations decoupled from any near-term earnings), the eventual unwind could drag sentiment even for the fundamentally sound names.

The Moon base story is real. It is also a long-dated thesis that will be used by momentum traders to chase aerospace stocks above fair value in the short term. The intelligent move is to own the picks-and-shovels names — semiconductors, materials, automation — rather than the headline-grabbing rocket builders.

Bottom line: SPX at 7,508 with 8 consecutive winning weeks and a new space economy catalyst. The bull case is intact but the margin of safety is lower than it was at 7,000. Our targets reflect continued upside if earnings breadth improves — with 7,750 as the three-month target and 7,800 as the year-end base case. The Moon is no longer science fiction. It is an investment frontier.

How We Forecast

We build our S&P 500 targets by synthesizing three inputs: (1) Wall Street consensus — the average of major institutional price targets from Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, UBS, HSBC, and JPMorgan; (2) historical seasonality — the S&P 500 has averaged +4.6% in years ending in '6' since 1950, and the May–July window has historically been favorable; (3) options market signals — the VIX term structure and implied move data tell us how much uncertainty the market is pricing near-term.

We weight these inputs by track record and relevance: bank consensus gets the most weight because it reflects fundamental analysis by dedicated equity strategy teams; seasonal patterns inform timing; implied vol calibrates our confidence interval. The result is a base case (most likely), a bull case (upside scenario), and a bear case (downside scenario) — not a single number that hides uncertainty.

What Could Derail the Bull Case

Three scenarios could push targets lower: a hard landing (unemployment spikes above 5.5%, GDP turns negative — base case moves to 6,800–7,000); a geopolitical shock (oil spike above $100 on Iran escalation, VIX re-tests 30+ — targets reset to 6,500–7,000); or a valuation contraction if the 10-year Treasury yield breaks above 5% (multiple compression removes 10–15% from fair value estimates).

On the upside, an AI productivity breakout — visible in Q2/Q3 earnings revisions — could accelerate the timeline to 8,000 or beyond. Fed rate cuts landing sooner than expected (first cut by September) would be an additional catalyst. The Moon base narrative, if it gains institutional traction, could expand the space-industrial investment thesis from a niche story to a multi-sector tailwind.
