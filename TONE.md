# Dependability — Tone & Style Guide

## Voice: The Dependability Research Desk

Dependability publishes independent financial research. Every article is written by the **Dependability Research Desk** — an institutional voice, not an individual byline. The product is analysis, not personality. Readers trust the work because it is rigorous, careful, and honest about uncertainty.

## Core Principles

### 1. Factual, Not Opinionated
Dependability writes about markets, not about political narratives around markets. When we discuss the effects of Federal Reserve policy, we report what the policy is, what the historical data shows, and what the plausible implications are. We do not editorialize about whether the policy is "right" or what it "should" be. When we cover S&P 500 dynamics, VIX behavior, or options strategies, we describe what the data shows and note where certainty is limited.

### 2. Acknowledge Uncertainty Honestly
Markets are uncertain. Every forecast we publish should explicitly state its confidence level and key assumptions. We do not use language that implies certainty we don't have. "Our base case expects..." not "The market will..." "Data suggests..." not "Clearly..."

### 3. No Political Framing
We do not frame financial or economic analysis through political lenses. We do not attribute market movements to political parties, ideological agendas, or social movements. We do not use language that carries political valance — "far-left," "far-right," " MAGA," "woke," "DEI" — in our analysis. We don't editorialize about regulatory policy by characterizing it as good or bad; we explain what it is and what its documented effects have been.

### 4. No Climate or Social Justice Content
Dependability covers financial research: S&P 500 dynamics, VIX, options strategies, macroeconomic indicators, real estate education. We do not cover climate change as a topic, do not frame investments through ESG or climate lenses, and do not include social justice commentary in our analysis. This is not a political statement — it is a scope statement. Our readers come to us for financial research, not for these topics. We stay in our lane.

### 5. Professional, Not Casual
We write like Time Magazine analysts, not Reddit commenters. Sentences are complete. Jargon is explained when first used. We don't use ALL CAPS, excessive exclamation points, or hyperbolic language. We don't say "this is huge" or "game-changing" without data backing it. Cool, precise, and professional.

## Tone Examples

### What We Sound Like (Correct)
> "The CBOE Volatility Index has risen 12 points over the past week, reaching levels not seen since early 2024. Historical data suggests that VIX readings above 25 are typically associated with elevated uncertainty in equity markets, though the relationship between VIX levels and actual market direction is non-linear. Our base case expects elevated volatility to persist through the end of Q2, with a range of outcomes dependent on upcoming Federal Reserve communications."

### What We Don't Sound Like (Incorrect)
> "The Fed's latest move is just another example of how out of touch Washington has become with ordinary investors. Markets are tanking because of political dysfunction, and until policymakers start listening to real people, things will only get worse."

> "The surge in VIX reflects deep structural uncertainties — the growing climate risks facing financial markets, the social instability caused by inequality, and the systemic failures of institutions that have failed to protect everyday Americans."

## Formatting Standards

- **Title case** for headlines and section headers (Time Magazine style)
- **No star ratings** — qualitative language only
- **Sourced claims** — every data point cites its source (CBOE, FRED, SEC, BLS, etc.)
- **Forecast disclosure** — every forecast article includes: confidence level, key assumptions, disclaimer that past performance does not guarantee future results
- **No emoji** — ever

## Source Standards

Every article should cite at minimum:
- **CBOE** — for VIX and options market data
- **FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data)** — for macroeconomic indicators
- **SEC** — for corporate filing data
- **BLS** — for employment and inflation data
- **Primary sources preferred** — use original data releases, not secondhand summaries

All data should be current to the most recent available release. No stale statistics. If data is more than 30 days old at time of publication, note that.
